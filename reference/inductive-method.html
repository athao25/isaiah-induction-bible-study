The Inductive Study Method

The one-page cheat sheet for every daily session. Print this.

Inductive means you start with the text itself and draw conclusions out of it, rather than coming to the text to confirm what you already think. Three steps, always in order:

1. Observation: "What does it say?"

Slow down. Read the passage at least twice. You are gathering facts, not meanings yet.

  • Who is speaking? To whom? Who is acted upon?
  • What is happening? What words repeat? What is compared or contrasted?
  • When / where? Any time markers ("in that day", "in the year that...")?
  • Connectors: circle "therefore", "but", "because", "so that". They carry the logic.
  • Tone and form: is this poetry or narrative? Accusation, promise, lament, praise?
Rule of thumb
If you have fewer than five written observations, you have not looked long enough. Boring passages are usually under-observed passages.

2. Interpretation: "What does it mean?"

One question rules this step: what did the author intend the original audience to understand? This is called the grammatical-historical method: meaning comes from the grammar of the words and the history of the situation, not from what a verse "means to me".

  • Context is king. Read the paragraph before and after. Then ask where the passage sits in the whole book (use the book map).
  • Scripture interprets Scripture. The clear passages govern the unclear. Follow cross-references, especially when the New Testament quotes Isaiah.
  • Watch the covenant. Isaiah constantly prosecutes Judah using the covenant of Deuteronomy: blessings for faithfulness, curses for rebellion. Ask "which covenant promise or curse is in play?"
  • Only now open helps. After you have a working answer, check the MacArthur Study Bible note or a commentary. Helps confirm or correct; they never replace your own look at the text.

3. Application: "What must change?"

Interpretation has one right answer; application has many. But application is not optional. James 1:22: be doers of the word, not hearers only.

  • What does this passage reveal about God: his character, his works, his promises?
  • What does it expose in me: sin to confess, truth to believe, command to obey?
  • Is there a promise here that should change how I face today?
Order matters
Never jump from reading straight to application. Application built on a misreading applies the wrong thing. Observe, interpret, then apply.
