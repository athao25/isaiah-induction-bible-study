Lesson 0010 · Unit 1: Chapters 1-6

Isaiah 5:8-30: Six Woes and a Darkening Land

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 5:8-30 (ESV, read first!) · The wild grapes, itemized

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 5:8-30 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. Longest reading so far; if pressed for time, read once slowly and mark every "woe" and every "therefore".

Recall first

Observation check

Yesterday the vineyard yielded wild grapes; today Isaiah holds each one up to the light. Strong observations include:

  • "Woe" six times (vv. 8, 11, 18, 20, 21, 22). A woe (Hebrew hoy) is a funeral cry, the word wailed over the dead. Isaiah pronounces it over people still walking around: the living dead.
  • "Therefore" judgments interleaved (vv. 13, 14, 24, 25). Each woe or cluster draws a sentence. Sin and consequence are stitched together through the whole poem.
  • Woe 1 (vv. 8-10): land-grabbers who "join house to house, who add field to field" until they dwell alone. Sentence: the hoarded estates yield almost nothing.
  • Woe 2 (vv. 11-17): dawn-to-dark drinkers with music at their feasts, "but they do not regard the deeds of the LORD". Sentence: exile "for lack of knowledge"; Sheol widens its throat.
  • Woe 3 (vv. 18-19): sinners who drag iniquity along with cart ropes and dare God: "let him speed his work that we may see it". Sin plus mockery of God's slowness.
  • Woe 4 (v. 20): "who call evil good and good evil... put darkness for light... bitter for sweet". The vocabulary of morality itself is inverted.
  • Woe 5 (v. 21): "wise in their own eyes, and shrewd in their own sight".
  • Woe 6 (vv. 22-23): heroes at drinking wine who "acquit the guilty for a bribe, and deprive the innocent of his right". Courtroom corruption, the direct opposite of the mishpat God sought in v. 7.
  • The whistle (v. 26): "he will whistle for them from the ends of the earth". God summons a distant nation the way a keeper calls bees.
  • The closing darkness (v. 30): growling like the sea, "distress and darkness... the light is darkened by its clouds". The chapter ends with the lights going out.

Interpretation

Verse 20: when a culture loses its dictionary

Woe to those who call evil good and good evil, who put darkness for light and light for darkness, who put bitter for sweet and sweet for bitter! Isaiah 5:20, ESV

The first three woes describe sins committed; the fourth describes something deeper, sin renamed. When greed is called ambition, drunkenness called celebration, and oppression called shrewd business, repentance becomes impossible, because the words needed to confess have been redefined. That is why this woe sits at the center of the six: moral inversion is the condition that lets all the others flourish unashamed. Note the order of collapse: first the deeds (woes 1-3), then the dictionary (woe 4), then the self-congratulation (woe 5), then the courts (woe 6). Cultures rot from practice to language to institutions.

The whistle: Assyria on a leash

Verses 26-30 describe an army: tireless, arrows sharp, wheels like the whirlwind, roaring like lions. Historically this is Assyria, the terror of the eighth century. But look who moves it: "He will raise a signal... and whistle for them" (v. 26). The superpower that thinks it marches for its own empire comes because God whistled. Isaiah will say it outright in 10:5: "Assyria, the rod of my anger". This is the doctrine of providence with its teeth showing: God sovereignly wields even pagan armies, without excusing their evil (10:12 will judge Assyria for the very campaign God summoned). Hold both truths; chapter 10 forces the issue.

Why the darkness matters structurally

Chapter 5 ends with no relief: land stripped, funeral cries, invading army, "darkness and distress" (v. 30). The vineyard song's sentence is being executed and no hope clause softens it. That blackout is deliberate staging. The very next verse you will read is 6:1: "In the year that King Uzziah died I saw the Lord sitting upon a throne". When the earthly lights go out, Isaiah is shown the throne that never flickers. Do not read chapter 6 as a fresh start; it is the answer to chapter 5's darkness.

Application

  • About God: he is patient (the mockers in v. 19 count on it) but not slow; the whistle proves every instrument of judgment is already at his lips. Scoffing at God's timetable is itself a woe.
  • About your words: v. 20 begins privately. Watch this week for the euphemisms you use for your own sins ("venting", "treating myself", "just being honest"). Renaming is the first anesthetic.
  • About discernment: calling evil good deserves a woe, but so does being "wise in your own eyes" (v. 21). Cultural critique that forgets woe 5 becomes woe 5. Diagnose the age on your knees, not from a pedestal.

Church prompt
Ask your pastor or group: where do you see our own culture, and our own church culture, renaming evil as good, and how do we say so without becoming the self-wise of verse 21? Listen for the second half of the question especially.
