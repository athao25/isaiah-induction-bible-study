Lesson 0006 · Unit 1: Chapters 1-6

Isaiah 2:6-22: A Day Against All That Is Proud

~25 min · Passage: Isaiah 2:6-22 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 2:6-22 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. Did you find the repeated refrain? You will need it below.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • The pivot (v. 6): "For you have rejected your people." After the glory of vv. 1-5, the reason Judah is not yet walking in the light: the land is "full" of the wrong things. Count the fulls: full of things from the east (pagan divination), full of silver and gold, full of horses and chariots, full of idols (vv. 6-8). Prosperity and paganism, stacked together.
  • The refrain (vv. 11, 17): "The haughty looks of man shall be brought low... and the LORD alone will be exalted in that day." Nearly word for word, twice. When a poem repeats itself like that, you have found its thesis.
  • The list of heights (vv. 12-16): cedars of Lebanon, oaks of Bashan, lofty mountains, high hills, every high tower, every fortified wall, ships of Tarshish. "The LORD of hosts has a day against all that is proud and lofty" (v. 12). Everything on the list is something ancient people boasted in: natural grandeur, military defense, commercial reach.
  • The caves (vv. 10, 19, 21): three times, people flee into rocks and holes "from before the terror of the LORD, and from the splendor of his majesty." Another repetition; another structural marker.
  • The idols' end (v. 20): in that day men throw the idols they made "to the moles and to the bats", the cave dwellers. The gods they crafted go underground with the people who trusted them.
  • The closing command (v. 22): "Stop regarding man in whose nostrils is breath, for of what account is he?" The whole poem lands on one imperative.

Interpretation

What is "the day of the LORD"?

"For the LORD of hosts has a day against all that is proud and lofty, against all that is lifted up; and it shall be brought low." Isaiah 2:12, ESV

Here the concept gets its classic definition. The day of the LORD is any moment when God steps visibly into history to put down human pride and exalt himself; the prophets use it for near historical judgments (Assyria's invasion, Babylon's fall) and for the final day those judgments prefigure. Each near "day" is a rehearsal and a warning of the last one. That is why Revelation 6:15-16 can reuse this very scene, kings and slaves hiding in caves, begging rocks to cover them from the face of the Lamb. Isaiah's poem telescopes: it lands first on proud Judah in the Assyrian crisis, and it keeps landing until the final judgment.

Why these targets?

Cedars, towers, ships, and mountains have committed no sin. They are stand-ins: every summit of human self-sufficiency, natural, military, and economic. The poem's logic is vertical. Man has built himself high; on that day, only one will be left standing tall: "the LORD alone will be exalted." Verse 22 draws the conclusion: stop regarding the creature whose whole life hangs on the breath in his nostrils. The idolatry of vv. 6-8 and the pride of vv. 11-17 are the same sin from two angles: trusting the made instead of the Maker.

Held together with vv. 1-5

Do not detach this poem from the mountain vision before it. The exaltation of the LORD's mountain (v. 2) and the humbling of every proud height (v. 12-17) are one event seen from two sides. Every rival summit must come down so that the nations stream to the true one. Judgment and hope are not competing messages in Isaiah; they are the same message.

Application

  • Inventory your high towers: list the things you would instinctively name if asked "why will I be okay?" Savings, skills, reputation, connections, health. None is evil; each becomes a tower when it carries the weight only God can. Name your tallest one to him today.
  • About fear: the men in the caves fear too late. Proverbs-style fear of the LORD now, reverent trust, is what makes the terror of that day unnecessary (Isa 8:13 will say exactly this).
  • Verse 22 as a habit: whose approval do you check compulsively? "Stop regarding man in whose nostrils is breath." Regard weighs nothing against the verdict of God.
