Lesson 0034 · Unit 4: Chapters 24-27

Isaiah 25: The Feast on the Mountain

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 25:1-12 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 25 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were asked to watch who the feast is for and what gets swallowed up; keep both answers beside you. This short chapter holds one of the biggest promises in the Old Testament.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • Judgment turns to song (25:1): "O LORD, you are my God; I will exalt you; I will praise your name." After the earth-emptying of chapter 24, the very next word is worship.
  • Plans formed of old (25:1): "plans formed of old, faithful and sure." The ruin of chapter 24 was not chaos winning; it was purpose keeping schedule.
  • The city again (25:2): "you have made the city a heap." The nameless world-city of 24:10 is still in view, now rubble.
  • A stronghold for the poor (25:4): "a stronghold to the poor... to the needy in his distress, a shelter from the storm." The same God who levels cities shelters the weak.
  • A feast for all peoples (25:6): "on this mountain the LORD of hosts will make for all peoples a feast of rich food... of well-aged wine." Note the guest list.
  • A veil over the nations (25:7): "he will swallow up on this mountain the covering that is cast over all peoples, the veil that is spread over all nations."
  • Death swallowed (25:8): "He will swallow up death forever; and the Lord GOD will wipe away tears from all faces." The biggest sentence in the chapter, maybe in the book so far.
  • We waited (25:9): "Behold, this is our God; we have waited for him... let us be glad and rejoice in his salvation." Faith looks like waiting that ends in gladness.
  • Moab underfoot (25:10-12): the chapter of feasting ends with Moab "trampled down... as straw is trampled down in a dunghill," and pride named again (25:11).

Interpretation

The apocalypse breaks into singing

Chapter 24 emptied the earth; chapter 25 opens with a solo voice praising God for it: "you have done wonderful things, plans formed of old, faithful and sure" (25:1). This is the structure of the whole section: judgment, then song (watch it happen again in 26:1 and 27:2). And the singer praises God for both halves of his work: the city made a heap (25:2) and the stronghold given to the poor (25:4). Isaiah will not let you choose one God, the judge or the shelter; the same storm that flattens the ruthless is the storm the needy are sheltered from. The remnant's song holds both.

The feast for all peoples

On this mountain the LORD of hosts will make for all peoples a feast of rich food, a feast of well-aged wine, of rich food full of marrow, of aged wine well refined. Isaiah 25:6, ESV

Read the guest list slowly: "all peoples." The nations judged in chapters 13-23, the earth emptied in 24, now seated at a table on Mount Zion. This is the mission thread of the book at full volume: the mountain the nations stream to (2:2-3), the root the nations seek (11:10), the refuge Moab was offered (16:1-5), now set with food and wine by the LORD himself. The host is God, the venue is Zion, the menu is his best ("well refined"), and the invitation is universal. Remember 24:23, the LORD reigning "before his elders" as glory, echoing the covenant meal of Exodus 24:9-11 where the elders "beheld God, and ate and drank." That mountain meal for seventy elders becomes a mountain meal for all peoples. Jesus picks up exactly this table: "many will come from east and west and recline at table with Abraham" (Matthew 8:11), and the marriage supper of the Lamb (Revelation 19:9) is this feast's final sitting. On our home lens this is where history is headed: not a shrinking guest list but a filling table, as the nations are discipled (Matthew 28:19). See the glossary on postmillennialism for the frame.

The swallower swallowed

He will swallow up death forever; and the Lord GOD will wipe away tears from all faces, and the reproach of his people he will take away from all the earth, for the LORD has spoken. Isaiah 25:8, ESV

First the veil: "the covering that is cast over all peoples" (25:7) is a funeral shroud, the thing draped over the dead. Every nation lives under it; every culture is a culture of people who die. Then the reversal, and savor the verb: death is the great swallower, the mouth that eats every generation (the grave "has enlarged its appetite," 5:14). Here God swallows the swallower. Not managed, not delayed: "swallow up death forever." And then the tenderest line in the book so far: the Lord GOD wiping tears off faces, one by one, like a parent. This is the Old Testament's clearest defeat-of-death text this early in redemptive history, and the New Testament knows it: Paul quotes 25:8 word for word at the climax of his resurrection chapter, "Death is swallowed up in victory" (1 Corinthians 15:54), locating its fulfillment at the resurrection of the body when Christ returns. Revelation 21:4 quotes the tears line for the new creation. Whatever else the eschatology lenses dispute, they agree here: this verse is about bodily resurrection, and it is certain, "for the LORD has spoken." The MacArthur Study Bible notes (your copy) place the feast in the millennial kingdom after Christ's return; note that Paul applies 25:8 to the resurrection at Christ's coming, and weigh how each reading fits the "all peoples" of 25:6.

Why Moab at the end?

The chapter closes strangely: feast, resurrection, and then Moab trampled in a dunghill (25:10-12). Why sour the song? Because Isaiah is answering a question the feast raises: is anyone not at the table? You know Moab's story from lesson 0026: offered refuge in Zion (16:1-4), a door already proven open by Ruth, and the offer refused by pride (16:6). Here the refrain returns: Moab spreads his hands "but the LORD will lay low his pompous pride" (25:11). Moab stands for every nation, and every heart, that prefers its pride to God's table. The feast is for all peoples; it is not for pride. Grace refused becomes judgment, and the two final images of the chapter, a laden table and a dunghill, are the only two endings there are.
