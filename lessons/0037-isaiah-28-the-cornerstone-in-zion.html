Lesson 0037 · Unit 5: Chapters 28-35

Isaiah 28: The Cornerstone in Zion

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 28:1-29 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 28 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were sent to watch what drink has done to Ephraim's crown and priests, and to mark the stone God lays at 28:16. Keep that verse marked; it carries the whole unit.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • A proud crown, fading (28:1-4): "Ah, the proud crown of the drunkards of Ephraim, and the fading flower of its glorious beauty." Samaria sat like a garland on a hill above a rich valley, and the garland is wilting.
  • A first-ripe fig (28:4): the city will be "like a first-ripe fig before the summer: when someone sees it, he swallows it as soon as it is in his hand." Judgment arrives that fast.
  • The true crown (28:5-6): "In that day the LORD of hosts will be a crown of glory, and a diadem of beauty, to the remnant of his people." Two crowns in five verses; only one fades.
  • Drunk clergy (28:7-8): "the priest and the prophet reel with strong drink... For all tables are full of filthy vomit, with no space left." The men responsible for God's word cannot see straight.
  • The mocking babble (28:9-10): "precept upon precept, precept upon precept, line upon line, line upon line, here a little, there a little." In Hebrew it is sing-song nonsense syllables, tsav latsav, qav laqav; the leaders are imitating Isaiah as a nursery teacher.
  • A foreign tongue (28:11-13): "by people of strange lips and with a foreign tongue the LORD will speak to this people." If they will not hear plain Hebrew, they will hear Assyrian.
  • Rest refused (28:12): "This is rest; give rest to the weary... yet they would not hear."
  • A covenant with death (28:14-15): the scoffers "who rule this people in Jerusalem" boast, "we have made a covenant with death... we have made lies our refuge."
  • The stone (28:16): "Behold, I am the one who has laid as a foundation in Zion, a stone, a tested stone, a precious cornerstone."
  • Hail and the plumb line (28:17-20): "hail will sweep away the refuge of lies," the covenant with death "will be annulled," and the famous proverb lands: "the bed is too short to stretch oneself on, and the covering too narrow to wrap oneself in."
  • Strange work (28:21): the LORD will rise "as on Mount Perazim; as in the Valley of Gibeon," battles where he once fought FOR Israel, but now "strange is his deed": he fights against his own city.
  • A farmer's parable (28:23-29): plowing, sowing, dill beaten with a stick, bread grain not crushed forever, "he is wonderful in counsel and excellent in wisdom."

Interpretation

Six woes: the shape of Unit 5

A new unit opens with a new sound: "Ah" (28:1), Hebrew hoy, the funeral cry you first heard in the vineyard chapter (5:8-23). Chapters 28-33 are built on six of these woes, a funeral procession for every false refuge Judah loved: woe on drunk Ephraim (28:1), woe on besieged Ariel (29:1), woe on those who hide plans from the LORD (29:15), woe on the stubborn children who run to Egypt (30:1), woe on those who trust horses and chariots (31:1), and a final woe on the destroyer himself (33:1). After the cosmic summit of chapters 24-27, Isaiah comes back down to street level and names the actual politics of his day: Assyria is coming, and Jerusalem's leaders are shopping for saviors. The first woe falls north. Samaria, capital of Ephraim, crowned a hill above a lush valley like a drinker's garland, and Isaiah sang its funeral shortly before Assyria swallowed it in 722 BC, quick as a first-ripe fig (28:4). Then, at verse 14, the camera swings south: "you scoffers, who rule this people in Jerusalem." The northern kingdom's fate is not a spectacle for Judah to watch; it is a mirror.

Baby talk, and the word they refused

Verses 9-13 record the only place in the book where you can hear Isaiah's opponents heckling him. "To whom will he teach knowledge?... Those who are weaned from the milk?" (28:9). And then the taunt itself: tsav latsav, tsav latsav, qav laqav, qav laqav (28:10), untranslatable nursery babble, roughly "blah upon blah, rule upon rule." The drunk clergy of verses 7-8 are mocking the prophet's preaching as kindergarten material, beneath sophisticated men who negotiate treaties. God's answer is terrifying in its precision: if plain Hebrew sounds like babble to you, then babble is what you will get, "by people of strange lips and with a foreign tongue the LORD will speak to this people" (28:11), the barked commands of Assyrian soldiers in your streets. Paul quotes exactly this verse at 1 Corinthians 14:21 to argue that unintelligible speech is a sign for unbelief, not a blessing; judgment, in Scripture, can arrive as a language you refused to understand. And do not miss what they turned down. The message they mocked was not complicated: "This is rest; give rest to the weary... yet they would not hear" (28:12). Hold that word "rest"; in chapter 30 it becomes your next memory verse, and the same tragedy: rest offered, rest refused.

The cornerstone

Behold, I am the one who has laid as a foundation in Zion, a stone, a tested stone, a precious cornerstone, of a sure foundation: "Whoever believes will not be in haste." Isaiah 28:16, ESV

Jerusalem's rulers had their security policy: "we have made a covenant with death, and with Sheol we have an agreement" (28:15), almost certainly a mocking name for their treaty with Egypt, guaranteed, they thought, to let the Assyrian flood pass them by. Isaiah calls it what it is: "we have made lies our refuge." Against their paperwork God sets masonry: one stone, laid by God himself, in Zion, and every word of the description is load-bearing. Tested: it has been proved; it holds. Precious: costly, not cheap fill. Cornerstone, sure foundation: the stone that squares the whole building; every other wall is trued against it. And then the promise: "Whoever believes will not be in haste," no panic, no scrambling for exits, the same quiet the kept mind of 26:3 enjoys. Faith here is not a feeling but a footing. The New Testament names the stone: Paul quotes 28:16 twice of Christ (Romans 9:33, 10:11), and Peter sets it beside Psalm 118:22, the rejected stone made head of the corner, in 1 Peter 2:6. The MacArthur Study Bible notes (your copy) read the stone as Messiah too; on this verse all our lenses stand together. Then verse 17 sends the storm: "hail will sweep away the refuge of lies," and the covenant with death is "annulled" (28:18), for death keeps no treaties. Verse 20 gives the whole chapter its proverb: "the bed is too short... the covering too narrow." Every refuge but the stone is a blanket that cannot cover you.

The farmer's parable: strange work, wise hands

The chapter could have ended at the "strange work" of verse 21, God rising at Perazim and Gibeon, battlefields where he once routed Israel's enemies (2 Samuel 5:20; Joshua 10:10-11), now roused against his own city; judgment on the church's own house is his "strange" and "alien" work, real but not his delight. Instead Isaiah closes with a farm (28:23-29). Does a farmer plow forever? No; plowing has a season, then sowing, each seed in its place. Is dill threshed with a heavy sledge? No; "dill is beaten out with a stick" (28:27), caraway with a rod, while bread grain is ground, but "he does not thresh it forever" (28:28). The parable answers the fear the chapter has raised: if God's own city is under the flail, is anything dosed, is anyone safe? Yes: the farmer's hands are wise. This is "measure by measure" (27:8) taught with plow and stick: God's judgments are seasonal, calibrated to the crop, never a tantrum, always tillage. "This also comes from the LORD of hosts; he is wonderful in counsel and excellent in wisdom" (28:29). The same counsel that swings the sledge at pride taps the dill with a stick, and both are harvest work.

Application

  • About God: he lays foundations rather than shoring up lies (28:16-17), and his discipline is a farmer's, not a flail's (28:23-29): seasonal, dosed, aimed at bread. Even his strange work serves the harvest.
  • About you: name your own covenant with death, the quiet deal you have cut with a fear: the savings figure, the connection, the plan B you actually trust to let the flood pass you by. Isaiah's word for every such refuge is "lies" (28:15).
  • Do: run the not-in-haste test this week. Each morning before news, email, or the feed, read 28:16 aloud once. Whoever believes will not be in haste; notice, by Friday, where your haste tells you your faith actually rests.
