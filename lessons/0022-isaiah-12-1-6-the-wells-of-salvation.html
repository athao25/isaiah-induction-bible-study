Lesson 0022 · Unit 2: Chapters 7-12

Isaiah 12:1-6: The Wells of Salvation

~25 min · Passage: Isaiah 12:1-6 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 12 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. It is only six verses, so read it a third time out loud: this chapter is a song, and songs are meant to be heard.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • "In that day" (vv. 1, 4): the phrase that stamped chapter 11's kingdom promises now introduces a song. The chapter is what the redeemed sing when the promises of chapters 7-11 come true.
  • Anger turned to comfort (v. 1): "though you were angry with me, your anger turned away, that you might comfort me." The singer does not deny the anger was real or deserved; the wonder is where it went.
  • An old song quoted (v. 2): "the LORD GOD is my strength and my song, and he has become my salvation." If that sounds familiar, it should: it is nearly word for word Exodus 15:2, the Song of the Sea.
  • Wells, plural (v. 3): "with joy you will draw water from the wells of salvation." Not a ration but a well field; drawing is ongoing, daily, joyful.
  • The song turns outward (vv. 4-5): "make known his deeds among the peoples... let this be made known in all the earth." Private rescue becomes public proclamation.
  • The Holy One is in the midst (v. 6): "Shout, and sing for joy, O inhabitant of Zion, for great in your midst is the Holy One of Israel." The title that has haunted the book since chapter 1 now lives at the center of a singing city.
  • Salvation three times (vv. 2, 2, 3): the Hebrew word yeshuah rings through the chapter like a refrain.

Interpretation

The gospel in one verse

You will say in that day: "I will give thanks to you, O LORD, for though you were angry with me, your anger turned away, that you might comfort me." Isaiah 12:1, ESV

Verse 1 is the gospel in miniature. God's anger against sin was real, personal ("angry with me"), and justified; nothing in the verse suggests it was a misunderstanding. But it "turned away", and the purpose clause is stunning: that you might comfort me. Wrath does not merely stop; it is replaced by comfort from the same God. Isaiah will later show how the anger turns away without injustice: it lands on a Servant who "was pierced for our transgressions" (53:5). For now, learn the shape: thanksgiving begins by naming honestly what we deserved and what we received instead.

Singing the exodus again

Verse 2 lifts its central line straight from Exodus 15:2, the song Israel sang on the far shore of the Red Sea. That is no accident: the last verse of chapter 11 promised "a highway from Assyria... as there was for Israel when they came up from the land of Egypt" (11:16). Chapter 12 is the song you sing on the far side of that highway: a second exodus deserves a second Song of the Sea. The "wells of salvation" (v. 3) belong to the same picture; a people who once feared drought in the wilderness now draw with joy from wells that do not fail. Centuries later, at the feast whose ritual each morning drew water while singing this very verse, Jesus stood up and cried, "If anyone thirsts, let him come to me and drink" (John 7:37-38). He was claiming to be the well.

Salvation gets a name

The word "salvation" (yeshuah) sounds three times in verses 2-3; it is the word behind the name Yeshua, Jesus, "the LORD saves" (Matthew 1:21), a connection worth noticing once and holding loosely. And saved people are given a job: "make known his deeds among the peoples... let this be made known in all the earth" (vv. 4-5). Salvation is not a private possession but a story to publish; the missionary horizon of 2:2-4 and 11:9-10 becomes the redeemed community's own assignment. Then the finale (v. 6): "great in your midst is the Holy One of Israel." This is Isaiah's signature title for God, the one the seraphim taught him to tremble before (6:3) and the one Judah despised (1:4). Unit 2 ends with that same Holy One dwelling in the midst of a shouting, singing Zion. Trace the unit's arc: Immanuel promised (ch. 7), the child given (ch. 9), the shoot reigning (ch. 11), and now the song (ch. 12). God with us, start to finish. Compare your MacArthur notes on chapter 12, and see the glossary on holiness if the title still feels strange.

Application

  • About God: his anger at his people has an expiration and a purpose: comfort (v. 1). If you are his, discipline is never the last word; singing is.
  • About you: worship is the goal of salvation, not an add-on to it. If your rescue has not yet turned into thanksgiving out loud, the story is stalled mid-chapter.
  • Do: read Isaiah 12 aloud once more as your own prayer (or sing it, if a tune suggests itself; no one is listening). Then review all five active memory verses from memory: 1:18, 6:3, 7:9b, 9:6, 11:9.
