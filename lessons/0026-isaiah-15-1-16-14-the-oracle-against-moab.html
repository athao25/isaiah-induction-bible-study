Lesson 0026 · Unit 3: Chapters 13-23

Isaiah 15:1-16:14: The Oracle Against Moab

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 15:1-16:14 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 15 and 16 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were asked to mark every line where the prophet's own emotion breaks through; keep that list beside you, it is the key to the whole oracle.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • Ruin in a night (15:1): "Because Ar of Moab is laid waste in a night... because Kir of Moab is laid waste in a night." Twice: the suddenness is the point.
  • A nation in mourning dress (15:2-3): every head bald, every beard shorn, sackcloth in the streets, weeping on the housetops. Town after town is named, the same catalog style as the Assyrian march in 10:28-32.
  • The prophet's own tears (15:5): "My heart cries out for Moab." Not Moab's heart: my heart. The judge's spokesman is weeping.
  • Blood on the waters (15:9): "the waters of Dibon are full of blood," and yet more is decreed: "a lion for those of Moab who escape."
  • Send the lamb (16:1): counsel to Moab: send tribute lambs "to the mount of the daughter of Zion": turn toward Jerusalem for shelter.
  • Shelter the outcasts (16:3-4): "let the outcasts of Moab sojourn among you; be a shelter to them from the destroyer." Zion is asked to take refugees in.
  • A sudden throne (16:5): "then a throne will be established in steadfast love, and on it will sit in faithfulness in the tent of David one who judges and seeks justice and is swift to do righteousness." A messianic flash inside a Moab oracle.
  • The root named (16:6): "We have heard of the pride of Moab, how proud he is! of his arrogance, his pride, and his insolence." Four pride-words in one verse.
  • Tears again, doubled (16:9-11): "I weep with the weeping of Jazer... my inner parts moan like a lyre for Moab." The oracle nearly ends in the prophet's sobbing.
  • A three-year clock (16:13-14): "In three years, like the years of a hired worker, the glory of Moab will be brought into contempt." A dated, checkable prophecy.

Interpretation

Who is Moab?

Moab is family. The nation descends from Lot, Abraham's nephew (Genesis 19:36-37), living just across the Dead Sea. The history is tangled: Balak king of Moab hired Balaam to curse Israel (Numbers 22-24), Moabite women drew Israel into sin at Peor (Numbers 25), and yet Ruth the Moabitess entered Israel by faith and became the great-grandmother of David (Ruth 4:17). So this oracle is not about a faceless enemy; it is about estranged kin. That background explains both the tears and the open door you are about to see.

The weeping judge

My heart cries out for Moab; her fugitives flee to Zoar, to Eglath-shelishiyah. Isaiah 15:5, ESV

Here is the feature you were sent to find: the oracle of judgment is soaked in the prophet's grief. "My heart cries out" (15:5); "I weep with the weeping of Jazer"; "my inner parts moan like a lyre" (16:9, 11). Isaiah does not gloat, and since the prophet speaks for God, the tears tell you something about God: "As I live, declares the Lord GOD, I have no pleasure in the death of the wicked, but that the wicked turn from his way and live" (Ezekiel 33:11). Judgment is real and it is decreed; and the Judge announces it in tears. Hold both, because Scripture does: this is the same book where the LORD's hand is stretched out and none can turn it back (14:27). The pattern reaches its full height when Jesus weeps over Jerusalem while pronouncing its doom (Luke 19:41-44). Contrast Philistia in last lesson, rejoicing over a fallen oppressor (14:29-31): God's people are taught to grieve where others gloat.

A throne in the tent of David

The strangest and best moment in the oracle is 16:1-5. Moab's refugees are counseled to send tribute to Zion and to seek shelter there, and Zion is counseled to take them in: "hide the outcasts... let the outcasts of Moab sojourn among you" (16:3-4). Then, without warning, the horizon opens: "a throne will be established in steadfast love... in the tent of David one who judges and seeks justice" (16:5). Even a Moab oracle bends toward Messiah. And Moab of all nations should believe it, because a Moabitess already found refuge under those wings: Ruth, who was told "the LORD repay you... under whose wings you have come to take refuge" (Ruth 2:12) and whose name stands in the royal line (Matthew 1:5). The door from Moab into the tent of David has been walked through before. On our home lens this is the mission thread of chapter 2 and 14:1-2 again: the nations are judged, and the same Zion that judges them stands open to their refugees; as the gospel runs, expect Moabs to keep coming in. Why then does Moab fall anyway? Verse 16:6 answers: pride, four words deep. The offer was real; pride refused it. The MacArthur Study Bible notes on these chapters (ch. 15, ch. 16) place the throne of 16:5 in the future millennial kingdom; note how the verse sits inside a present-tense counsel to shelter refugees, and weigh the readings.

Three years, like a hired worker

The oracle closes with a timestamp: "In three years, like the years of a hired worker, the glory of Moab will be brought into contempt" (16:14). A hired worker counts his contract to the day; the prophecy is that precise and that checkable. Isaiah keeps doing this (7:8, 16; 8:4; 20:3; 37:30; 38:5): datable words in public, where failure would be exposed. Deuteronomy 18:22 made accuracy the test of a prophet; Isaiah signs his oracles with dates because his Sender keeps schedules.

Application

  • About God: he decrees judgment and weeps over it, and neither cancels the other (15:5; Ezekiel 33:11). Any picture of God that keeps only the tears or only the decree is not the God of this oracle.
  • About you: practice grieving where you are tempted to gloat. When someone who wronged you falls, or the public figure you oppose is disgraced, Philistia's party (14:29-31) is closed to you; Isaiah's lyre-ache is the model. Pray for one such person by name today.
  • Do: review all five active memory verses (1:18, 6:3, 7:9b, 9:6, 11:9) out loud from memory, references before and after. The recall quiz next lesson will draw on them.
