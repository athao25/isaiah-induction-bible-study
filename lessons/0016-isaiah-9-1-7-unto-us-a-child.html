Lesson 0016 · Unit 2: Chapters 7-12

Isaiah 9:1-7: The Government upon His Shoulder

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 9:1-7 (ESV, read first!) · Memory verse 4 activates today

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 9:1-7 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. This is one of the summits of the whole Bible; do not take the cable car.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • The hinge from darkness to light (v. 2): chapter 8 ended with people thrust into "thick darkness" (8:22). Chapter 9 opens: "the people who walked in darkness have seen a great light." The paragraph break in your Bible hides the drama; this is one continuous movement from night to dawn.
  • Named geography (v. 1): Zebulun, Naphtali, "Galilee of the nations". Not generic; two specific tribal regions in the north.
  • Joy piled up (v. 3): joy "as with joy at the harvest, as they are glad when they divide the spoil". Two comparisons: farm and battlefield.
  • Three "for" clauses (vv. 4, 5, 6): each verse opens with "For", stacking reasons for the joy. Circle them; they carry the logic. The climactic reason is v. 6: "For to us a child is born."
  • "The day of Midian" (v. 4): a history reference. Hold it for interpretation.
  • Burned boots (v. 5): the boots of tramping warriors and blood-rolled garments are fuel for fire. War equipment is not stored; it is destroyed.
  • Born and given (v. 6): "to us a child is born, to us a son is given". Two different verbs. Then government on his shoulder, then four throne names.
  • No end (v. 7): the increase of his government and of peace has no end, on "the throne of David", upheld "with justice and with righteousness from this time forth and forevermore".
  • The guarantee (v. 7): "The zeal of the LORD of hosts will do this." Not human effort; God's own jealousy for his promise.

Interpretation

Why Zebulun and Naphtali?

In 732 BC Tiglath-pileser of Assyria tore away exactly these northern regions first (2 Kings 15:29). The lands first into the darkness are promised to be first into the light. Now read Matthew 4:13-16: Jesus leaves Nazareth and settles in Capernaum, "in the territory of Zebulun and Naphtali", and Matthew quotes this very passage as fulfilled. The great light of Isaiah 9 is not a metaphor for national recovery; it is a Galilean ministry that began seven centuries later, in the precise postcode Isaiah named. Predictive prophecy at street-level resolution.

The day of Midian

Gideon's three hundred with torches and trumpets (Judges 7): a victory engineered by God so that Israel could not say "my own hand has saved me" (Judg 7:2). By choosing that battle as his comparison, Isaiah tells you in advance how this deliverance works: God wins it alone, and his people carry lamps, not swords. The burned boots of v. 5 agree: when this King ends war, war stays ended.

Born, and given

A child is born: real humanity, a baby with a birthday. A son is given: he comes from outside, from God, a gift that already existed to give. The two verbs hold the two natures of Christ in seed form. Then the four throne names, each a pair of words:

  • Wonderful Counselor: "wonderful" in Isaiah is miracle vocabulary (cf. Judg 13:18); his counsel needs no advisors and never fails.
  • Mighty God (Hebrew el gibbor): the decisive name. Some claim it means only "godlike hero". But turn one page: in Isaiah 10:21 the remnant returns "to the mighty God", the identical phrase, and there it is unmistakably Yahweh himself. Isaiah uses his own dictionary: the child on David's throne is God. James White presses exactly this argument for the deity of Christ, and it holds.
  • Everlasting Father: not a confusion of the Trinity's persons; the Son is not the Father. In royal idiom a king is "father" to his people (cf. Isa 22:21). This King fathers, protects, and provides for his people forever.
  • Prince of Peace: his administration is shalom; the burned boots of v. 5 are his policy.

A government that only increases

Of the increase of his government and of peace there will be no end, on the throne of David and over his kingdom, to establish it and to uphold it with justice and with righteousness from this time forth and forevermore. Isaiah 9:7, ESV

When does this reign begin? Gabriel answers Mary in Luke 1:32-33 with Isaiah 9 in his mouth: "the Lord God will give to him the throne of his father David... and of his kingdom there will be no end." Peter preaches at Pentecost that God seated the risen Jesus on David's promised throne (Acts 2:30-36). On our home lens (Durbin, postmillennial), that means the reign is inaugurated and increasing now: "of the increase... no end" describes the gospel's growth through history, mustard-seed style, until the nations stream in. MacArthur (premillennial) reads the Davidic throne as awaiting a future earthly millennium after Christ returns. White (amillennial) agrees the reign is present but locates it as Christ's heavenly session consummated at the last day. All three agree on the person; the fork is the timetable of the throne. Watch the text as we go; Isaiah will keep feeding this discussion at 11:9 and 65:17.

Application

  • About God: "the zeal of the LORD of hosts will do this." The kingdom does not survive on your zeal; it advances on his. Pray big prayers accordingly.
  • About you: this is a Christmas text, and it is July. Good. It was never a seasonal decoration; it is a standing government. Name your heaviest current anxiety, then set it next to a kingdom whose increase has no end, and ask which one is temporary.
  • Do: the people in darkness saw a great light because the light moved toward them (Matt 4:13). Move toward someone sitting in darkness this week with the good news of this King.
