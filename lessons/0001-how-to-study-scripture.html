Lesson 0001 · Orientation

How to Study Scripture

~25 min · No passage prerequisite · The one skill every other lesson builds on

Before Isaiah, the tool. This course uses inductive study: you draw meaning out of the text instead of reading your assumptions into it. Today you learn the three steps and practice them on two verses of Isaiah.

Why method matters

Scripture is God speaking (2 Tim 3:16). If God has spoken, then the meaning of a passage is not up to us; our job is to hear what he actually said. That conviction, common to MacArthur, White, and Durbin and to the whole Reformed tradition, is why they all preach expositionally: verse by verse, letting the text set the agenda. You are about to do the same thing at a personal scale, 20 minutes at a time, for 66 chapters.

The three steps

StepQuestionDiscipline
1. ObservationWhat does it say?Look long. List facts: who, what, when, repeated words, connectors, contrasts.
2. InterpretationWhat does it mean?What did the author intend the original audience to understand? Context first; Scripture interprets Scripture.
3. ApplicationWhat must change?What does this reveal about God? What in me must be confessed, believed, obeyed?

The order is not decoration. Skip observation and your interpretation floats free of the text. Skip interpretation and your application applies something the passage never said. The full cheat sheet lives at reference: inductive method; it is your daily template from tomorrow on.

One rule for your helps
You own the MacArthur Study Bible in Olive Tree. Excellent tool, wrong first move. Always make your own observations before opening any note or commentary. Helps confirm and correct your reading; they must never replace it.

Practice: Isaiah 1:2-3

Read this slowly, twice. Then, before scrolling on, write down at least four observations on paper.

Hear, O heavens, and give ear, O earth; for the LORD has spoken: "Children have I reared and brought up, but they have rebelled against me. The ox knows its owner, and the donkey its master's crib, but Israel does not know, my people do not understand." Isaiah 1:2-3, ESV

Done writing? Compare with what careful observation surfaces:

  • Who speaks: "the LORD has spoken". Isaiah's words are God's words; the book opens by settling its own authority.
  • Who is addressed: heavens and earth, summoned like witnesses in a courtroom. (Moses called the same witnesses in Deut 30:19 and 32:1; Isaiah is reopening Moses' covenant case. Interpretation will make much of this.)
  • The relationship: "Children have I reared". The charge is not rule-breaking first; it is a son's rebellion against a father.
  • The contrast: ox and donkey vs Israel. Dumb animals know who feeds them; God's own people do not know him. The comparison is designed to sting.
  • Repetition: "does not know... do not understand". The problem is portrayed as ignorance of the heart, not lack of information.

Notice what happened: none of that required a commentary. It required slowing down. That is the skill this whole course trains.
