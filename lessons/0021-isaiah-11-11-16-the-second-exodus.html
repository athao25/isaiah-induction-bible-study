Lesson 0021 · Unit 2: Chapters 7-12

Isaiah 11:11-16: The Second Exodus

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 11:11-16 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 11:11-16 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were asked to watch for the phrase "a second time", for a highway, and for echoes of the exodus from Egypt. What earlier event is this passage replaying?

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • "A second time" (v. 11): "In that day the Lord will extend his hand yet a second time to recover the remnant that remains of his people." If there is a second recovery, there was a first. Which was it?
  • The list of lands (v. 11): Assyria, Egypt, Pathros, Cush, Elam, Shinar, Hamath, the coastlands of the sea. Not one direction but every direction; the scattered are everywhere.
  • The signal again (v. 12): "He will raise a signal for the nations and will assemble the banished of Israel." The same banner word as verse 10; there the nations inquire, here the outcasts gather.
  • The old feud healed (v. 13): "The jealousy of Ephraim shall depart... Ephraim shall not be jealous of Judah, and Judah shall not harass Ephraim." The civil war that split the kingdom after Solomon simply ends.
  • One people on the move (v. 14): reunited Israel swoops on Philistia, plunders "the people of the east"; former rivals act as one body.
  • The sea dried up (v. 15): "the LORD will utterly destroy the tongue of the Sea of Egypt, and will wave his hand over the River"; the water obstacle is split into seven passable streams.
  • The highway (v. 16): "there will be a highway from Assyria for the remnant... as there was for Israel when they came up from the land of Egypt." Isaiah names his own source: this is exodus language, on purpose.

Interpretation

The exodus is the Bible's grammar of salvation

And there will be a highway from Assyria for the remnant that remains of his people, as there was for Israel when they came up from the land of Egypt. Isaiah 11:16, ESV

Verse 16 tells you how to read the whole paragraph: "as there was for Israel when they came up from the land of Egypt." When God wants to describe a future salvation, he retells his most famous past one. A hand extended against the sea (v. 15, echoing Exodus 14:21), water split for the people to cross dry-shod, a road out of the house of bondage: this is typology working at full scale. The first exodus becomes the pattern, the "type", of a greater rescue: not out of one country but out of every country on the list, wherever the remnant has been scattered. And the New Testament keeps the pattern running: on the mount of transfiguration, Moses himself stands with Jesus and they speak "of his departure, which he was about to accomplish at Jerusalem" (Luke 9:31), and the Greek word for departure is exodos. The cross is the greater exodus; the sea that gets split is death itself.

Whose regathering is this?

Here the teachers divide along the same line as last lesson. MacArthur's note on this passage (your study Bible, ch. 11) reads verse 11 as a still-future, national regathering of ethnic Israel to her land in the millennial kingdom, and he can point to the plain geography: real place names, real dispersion. Take the strength of that seriously: Isaiah's first hearers would have heard actual countries. On our home lens, follow the banner. Verse 12's "signal for the nations" is the same signal as verse 10, and Paul has already told us (Romans 15:12) who is gathering to it now: Jew and Gentile together, hoping in the root of Jesse. The first-stage fulfillment was literal enough, the return from Babylon; but the "second time" keeps widening. In the gospel age the Lord's hand is extended to recover his scattered ones "from the four corners of the earth" (v. 12), and the church is that one assembled people: "he himself is our peace, who has made us both one" (Ephesians 2:14), which is verse 13's healed feud written across a bigger map. Ephraim and Judah, Jew and Gentile, old enemies made one under one banner. Hold the readings honestly; both agree that God keeps covenant with the scattered and loses none of them.

Why the healed jealousy matters

Verse 13 deserves a minute on its own. The deepest wound in Israel's story was not inflicted by Assyria; it was self-inflicted, the split of one kingdom into two rival ones (1 Kings 12), two centuries of cold and hot war between brothers. Isaiah promises that under the shoot of Jesse the jealousy "shall depart", not be managed, not simmer politely: depart. Reunion under the Messiah is not a side effect of salvation; it is part of its definition. A saved people that still despises each other is a contradiction in terms.

Application

  • About God: his hand goes out "a second time" (11:11). God is not a one-rescue God; the same arm that saved before is extended still, and no scattered child of his is beyond its reach.
  • About you: verse 13 heals a generations-old rivalry. Is there an Ephraim-and-Judah split you have made peace with as permanent: in your church, your family, between believers you have written off? Under this King, old feuds are not furniture; name one and pray for its departure.
  • Do: recite memory verse 5 (Isaiah 11:9) from memory 5 times, reference before and after, then run all five verses (1:18, 6:3, 7:9b, 9:6, 11:9) out loud in order.
