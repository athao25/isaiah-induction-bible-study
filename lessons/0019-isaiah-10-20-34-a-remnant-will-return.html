Lesson 0019 · Unit 2: Chapters 7-12

Isaiah 10:20-34: Shear-jashub's Promise

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 10:20-34 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 10:20-34 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. A boy's strange name from chapter 7 becomes a sermon in this passage; you were asked to find it.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • The lean reversed (v. 20): "the remnant of Israel... will no more lean on him who struck them, but will lean on the LORD, the Holy One of Israel, in truth." Ahaz's whole policy (leaning on Assyria, ch. 7) is named and repented of in one line.
  • The boy's name preached (v. 21): "A remnant will return." In Hebrew that is shear-jashub, the exact name of the son Isaiah carried to meet Ahaz in 7:3. The walking signpost becomes a promise.
  • Return to whom (v. 21): "to the mighty God", el gibbor, the identical title given to the child of 9:6. Mark the connection; it is not an accident.
  • Sand and severity (vv. 22-23): though Israel be "as the sand of the sea" (Abraham's promise vocabulary, Gen 22:17), only a remnant returns; "destruction is decreed, overflowing with righteousness."
  • "Therefore do not fear" (vv. 24-27): the application God himself draws: do not fear the Assyrian's rod, "for in a very little while my fury will come to an end." Two history echoes: Midian at the rock of Oreb (Gideon again, as in 9:4), and the staff over the sea (the exodus).
  • The march (vv. 28-32): a town-by-town itinerary: Aiath, Migron, Michmash, Geba; "Ramah trembles, Gibeah of Saul has fled"; closer and closer until "this very day he will halt at Nob and shake his fist at the mount of the daughter of Zion." The camera zooms in one village at a time.
  • The felling (vv. 33-34): "the Lord GOD of hosts will lop the boughs with terrifying power... the majestic forest will fall by the Majestic One." Assyria, the great forest, is cut down at the very gates.

Interpretation

A name becomes a doctrine

In 7:3 Shear-jashub was a silent warning stapled to Isaiah's side: judgment is coming, only a remnant survives. Here the same name turns its bright side outward: a remnant will return, leaning on Yahweh in truth, and the God they return to is el gibbor: the very name the child of 9:6 carries. Isaiah is stitching his book together: the remnant does not merely survive judgment; it comes home to the Messiah. The remnant theme you first met at 1:9 and 6:13 now has an address.

Paul reads this passage

For though your people Israel be as the sand of the sea, only a remnant of them will return. Destruction is decreed, overflowing with righteousness. Isaiah 10:22, ESV

Romans 9:27-28 quotes these very verses to explain Paul's own day: not all physical Israel is saved, but God's word has not failed, because the promise always ran through a believing remnant. On our home lens, that remnant flows into the one olive tree of Romans 11: believing Jews and grafted-in Gentiles, one people leaning on the Holy One in truth; and the same Romans 11 holds out fullness ahead as the gospel runs. MacArthur reads Romans 11 as also promising a distinct future national restoration of Israel; White stands closer to the one-olive-tree reading. All agree on this much: the remnant is defined by faith, not bloodline alone, and it returns to the Mighty God.

Why the list of village names?

Verses 28-32 are cinema. Each town named is a few miles closer to Jerusalem; the pass at Michmash, the fled villages, Nob on the last ridge, close enough to shake a fist at Zion. Isaiah builds the terror deliberately so that verses 33-34 land with full weight: at the exact moment the axe-empire reaches the gates, the real Woodsman fells the forest (fulfilled in 37:36). And one more seam: chapter 10 ends with a forest cut down never to rise. Chapter 11 opens with a different felled tree: "there shall come forth a shoot from the stump of Jesse." Assyria's stump is dead forever; David's stump carries a living shoot. That contrast is next lesson, and it is one of the great pivots of the book.

Application

  • About God: "in a very little while my fury will come to an end" (v. 25). God's discipline of his people has a set expiration; his furies keep his schedule, not yours. Wait for it in hope, not dread.
  • About you: name the thing you lean on that has actually struck you (v. 20): the approval, the habit, the security that wounds you even as you grip it. Leaning on the LORD "in truth" starts with un-leaning somewhere specific.
  • Do: memorize nothing new today; instead review all four active memory verses (1:18, 6:3, 7:9b, 9:6) from memory, out loud, references included.
