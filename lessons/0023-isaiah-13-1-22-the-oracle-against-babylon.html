Lesson 0023 · Unit 3: Chapters 13-23

Isaiah 13:1-22: The Oracle Against Babylon

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 13:1-22 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 13 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were asked to note the heading word, whom the oracle concerns, and how big the language gets; have those three ready.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • A new heading (v. 1): "The oracle concerning Babylon which Isaiah the son of Amoz saw." The word "oracle" (massa) literally means a burden, something heavy the prophet must carry and deliver. Eleven such headings structure chapters 13-23.
  • The wrong empire? (v. 1): the oracle targets Babylon, but in Isaiah's day Assyria is the superpower and Babylon is a restless province under its thumb. Isaiah is naming a giant a century before it stands up.
  • An army mustered by God (vv. 2-5): "I myself have commanded my consecrated ones... the LORD of hosts is mustering a host for battle." The army marching on Babylon takes its orders from Yahweh.
  • The day of the LORD (vv. 6, 9): "Wail, for the day of the LORD is near"; "Behold, the day of the LORD comes, cruel, with wrath and fierce anger." The phrase brackets the chapter's center.
  • The sky goes out (v. 10): "the stars of the heavens and their constellations will not give their light; the sun will be dark at its rising." Judgment described as the universe being switched off.
  • People become scarce as gold (v. 12): "I will make people more rare than fine gold, and mankind than the gold of Ophir."
  • The Medes named (v. 17): "Behold, I am stirring up the Medes against them", a people with no interest in silver or gold; they cannot be bought off.
  • Sodom's twin (vv. 19-22): "Babylon, the glory of kingdoms... will be like Sodom and Gomorrah when God overthrew them. It will never be inhabited"; only desert creatures will live in its palaces.

Interpretation

Why a whole unit against other nations?

Chapters 13-23 can feel like a strange detour: eleven oracles aimed at nations most of whom would never read them. But remember who does read them: little Judah, wedged between empires, forever tempted either to fear the nations or to trust them (Ahaz's Assyria policy in chapter 7 was exactly that). The oracles preach one sustained point: the Holy One of Israel governs every nation on the map, so his people need neither dread the empires nor lean on them. Setting Babylon first is deliberate; by the book's second half, Babylon (not Assyria) will be the great enemy, and chapters 13-14 plant the flag early: that empire's fall is already decreed. The day of the LORD and the falling sky

For the stars of the heavens and their constellations will not give their light; the sun will be dark at its rising, and the moon will not shed its light. Isaiah 13:10, ESV

"The day of the LORD" names any day God steps into history to judge and save; there are many such days, and they all rehearse the final one. Notice what the prophets do with the sky: when a world power falls, its sun, moon, and stars go dark. This is the Bible's idiom for the collapse of a political universe; lights out for the powers that seemed as fixed as the heavens. Babylon's fall was not accompanied by literal astronomical failure, yet verse 10 was true. Keep this idiom in your pocket: Jesus quotes these very images in Matthew 24:29, and on our home lens he is using them the same way Isaiah did, for the fall of a city within history (Jerusalem, AD 70). Many readers, MacArthur among them, take Matthew 24 as describing the end of history instead; that is a fair reading to weigh, and the glossary maps the options. Either way, the grammar of the imagery is learned here first.

Named a century early

Babylon rose to crush Assyria in 612-605 BC and fell to Cyrus's army, Medes prominent in it, in 539 BC (Daniel 5 narrates the last night). Isaiah names the Medes as the instrument roughly 150 years ahead (v. 17); this is prophecy's long reach, and one reason critics later insisted a second author must have written parts of Isaiah, an issue we will face squarely in the chapter 40s. The desolation oracle (vv. 19-22) came true on a longer fuse: Babylon declined for centuries until it was what it remains, an uninhabited ruin in Iraq. And the city became a type: Revelation 17-18 borrows Babylon's name for the world's whole city-in-rebellion, and sings a funeral song over it with Isaiah's tune. Your MacArthur notes on chapter 13 read the chapter as also describing a rebuilt end-time Babylon destroyed at Christ's return; weigh that against the view taken here, that Babylon functions as the archetype: every proud empire that sets itself against God falls as Babylon fell, until the last one does.

Application

  • About God: he schedules the rise and fall of superpowers, naming instruments before the actors are born (v. 17). No empire, market, or movement is outside his calendar.
  • About you: name the "Babylon" of your day, the power you are most tempted to fear or to envy. Isaiah 13 says its sun is already scheduled to go dark; fear and envy are both bad investments.
  • Do: review your five memory verses, and linger on 11:9: the earth ends up full of the knowledge of the LORD, not of Babylon.
