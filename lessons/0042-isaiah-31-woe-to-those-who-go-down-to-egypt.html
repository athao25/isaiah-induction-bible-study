Lesson 0042 · Unit 5: Chapters 28-35

Isaiah 31: Down to Egypt for Help

~25 min · Passage: Isaiah 31:1-9 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 31 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were sent to watch what horses and chariots stand for, and to find the two back-to-back pictures of God in verses 4-5: a lion and hovering birds. Keep both marked; they carry the chapter.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • The woe named (31:1): "Woe to those who go down to Egypt for help and rely on horses, who trust in chariots because they are many and in horsemen because they are very strong."
  • The double negative (31:1): "but do not look to the Holy One of Israel or consult the LORD." Two things they never did.
  • A dry aside (31:2): "And yet he is wise and brings disaster; he does not call back his words." God, too, has a strategy department.
  • The category verse (31:3): "The Egyptians are man, and not God, and their horses are flesh, and not spirit."
  • Shared collapse (31:3): "When the LORD stretches out his hand, the helper will stumble, and he who is helped will fall, and they will all perish together."
  • The lion (31:4): a lion growling over his prey, and "when a band of shepherds is called out against him," he "is not terrified by their shouting."
  • The birds (31:5): "Like birds hovering, so the LORD of hosts will protect Jerusalem; he will protect and deliver it; he will spare and rescue it." Count the verbs.
  • A call home (31:6): "Turn to him from whom people have deeply revolted, O children of Israel."
  • Idols thrown away (31:7): "in that day everyone shall cast away his idols of silver and his idols of gold, which your own hands have sinfully made for you."
  • A sword not of man (31:8): "And the Assyrian shall fall by a sword, not of man; and a sword, not of man, shall devour him." Said twice in one verse.
  • Panic and fire (31:9): "his officers desert the standard in panic, declares the LORD, whose fire is in Zion, and whose furnace is in Jerusalem."

Interpretation

The fifth woe: arithmetic versus trust

Listen to the reasons Judah's envoys gave for the Egyptian alliance, because Isaiah quotes them: chariots "because they are many," horsemen "because they are very strong" (31:1). Counting is the natural religion of the frightened. Add up the divisions, weigh the tonnage, and the side with the bigger number wins; it feels like realism, and Isaiah calls it a woe. The diagnosis is the double negative at the end of the verse: they "do not look to the Holy One of Israel or consult the LORD." Not that they cursed him; they simply left him off the list of relevant factors. No look, no question, no prayer before the treaty was signed. Then comes one of the driest lines in the book: "And yet he is wise" (31:2). You went to Egypt for expertise; it turns out God also has some. He "brings disaster; he does not call back his words." Pharaoh's counselors revise their advice when circumstances shift; this strategist has never once had to retract a sentence. The woe is not against planning. It is against planning that has quietly concluded God is not one of the powers that matter.

Flesh, and not spirit

The Egyptians are man, and not God, and their horses are flesh, and not spirit. When the LORD stretches out his hand, the helper will stumble, and he who is helped will fall, and they will all perish together. Isaiah 31:3, ESV

This is the theological center of the chapter, and notice what kind of argument it is: not a comparison but a classification. Isaiah does not say Egypt is weaker than advertised; Egypt's cavalry may be everything the brochures claim. He says Egypt is on the wrong shelf. There are two categories of reality: God and spirit on one side, man and flesh on the other, and no quantity of the second category adds up to the first. A million horses are still flesh; flesh multiplied is just more flesh. Judah's sin was a category error: they filed Egypt under "savior," a slot only spirit can fill. And the end of the verse shows how such trust fails: one outstretched hand, and "the helper will stumble, and he who is helped will fall, and they will all perish together." Shared trust means shared collapse; rope yourself to a falling climber and you learn what he is made of on the way down. Jesus draws the same two-shelf line: "the flesh is no help at all" (John 6:63). The question chapter 31 asks of every refuge is not "how strong?" but "which category?"

The lion and the birds

Like birds hovering, so the LORD of hosts will protect Jerusalem; he will protect and deliver it; he will spare and rescue it. Isaiah 31:5, ESV

Having taken Egypt's protection away, God describes his own, in two pictures set deliberately back to back. First the lion (31:4): growling over his prey while "a band of shepherds is called out against him," and utterly unmoved by their shouting. However you count the shepherds, the lion is not doing arithmetic; the LORD of hosts stands over Mount Zion and no coalition of nations can shout him off it. Unbudgeable. Then, without a breath, the birds (31:5): wings beating over the nest, the picture every farm child knew of a mother bird hovering over her young. And the verbs pile up four deep, as if one or two could not carry the weight: "protect... deliver... spare... rescue." Fierce as a lion, tender as wings; the same God, the same city, the same moment. And Isaiah has chosen his words to smell of a particular night: hovering, sparing, the LORD himself passing over to protect. That is Passover language (Exodus 12:23, 27), when the destroyer passed through Egypt but the LORD stood guard at blood-marked doors. The nation trying to hire Egypt's protection is offered the protection Egypt's firstborn never had. The MacArthur Study Bible notes (your copy) point out the same connection; some pictures are too exact to be accidents.

A sword, not of man

The chapter ends with a promise so important Isaiah says it twice in one verse: "the Assyrian shall fall by a sword, not of man; and a sword, not of man, shall devour him" (31:8). No Egyptian cavalry, no Judean garrison, no human weapon at all will get the credit when Assyria goes down; his young men will be put to forced labor, his officers will "desert the standard in panic" (31:9). Hold this promise with both hands, because Isaiah is writing a check the narrative will cash: in 37:36 the angel of the LORD walks through the Assyrian camp by night, and 185,000 soldiers never see morning. Prophecy first, history after, so that when it happens nobody can misfile the credit. And the last line of the chapter gives the promise its ground: the LORD, "whose fire is in Zion, and whose furnace is in Jerusalem" (31:9). God's own fire dwells in the city, which is why no invader's torch will. But notice the edge on that comfort: a furnace in your own city warms the trusting and terrifies the treacherous, and Isaiah will make that edge explicit at 33:14, when the sinners in Zion ask, "Who among us can dwell with the consuming fire?" Keep that question in your pocket; it is two chapters away.

Memory verse 8: Isaiah 30:15, day three
Recite 30:15 from memory 5 times today, reference before and after. Then notice: chapter 31 is that verse negatively illustrated, horses chosen over rest, alliances over quietness. While you are at it, review verse 3 (Isaiah 7:9b), the same lesson from Ahaz's day: "If you are not firm in faith, you will not be firm at all." Schedule and method: memory verses.

Application

  • About God: he is unbudgeable as a lion over his prey (31:4), tender as wings over a nest (31:5), and categorically unlike every helper you can hire (31:3). The helpers are flesh; he is spirit, and he does not call back his words.
  • About you: name what you trust "because they are many": followers, dollars, options, contingencies, contacts. Then ask the 31:1 question honestly: in your last hard decision, whom did you consult, and was the LORD anywhere on the list?
  • Do: take one live fear. Write down the helpers you have stacked against it. This week, formally consult God first: pray Isaiah 31:1-3 over the list, ask him which helpers are flesh masquerading as spirit, and cut one.
