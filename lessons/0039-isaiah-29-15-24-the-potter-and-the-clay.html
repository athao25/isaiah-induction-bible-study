Lesson 0039 · Unit 5: Chapters 28-35

Isaiah 29:15-24: The Potter and the Clay

~25 min · Passage: Isaiah 29:15-24 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 29:15-24 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were sent to watch people hiding their plans deep from the LORD, and a pot talking back to its potter; keep 29:16 marked. It is one sentence you will meet again in Romans.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • Hiding from the LORD (29:15): "Ah, you who hide deep from the LORD your counsel, whose deeds are in the dark, and who say, 'Who sees us? Who knows us?'" The third woe of the unit.
  • Upside down (29:16): "You turn things upside down! Shall the potter be regarded as the clay...?" The roles have been reversed.
  • The pot's two sentences (29:16): the thing made says of its maker, "He did not make me," and "He has no understanding." Absurd on purpose.
  • A little while (29:17): "Is it not yet a very little while until Lebanon shall be turned into a fruitful field...?" The forest becomes farmland.
  • The deaf hear a book (29:18): "In that day the deaf shall hear the words of a book, and out of their gloom and darkness the eyes of the blind shall see." Remember last lesson's sealed book.
  • Joy for the meek (29:19): "The meek shall obtain fresh joy in the LORD, and the poor among mankind shall exult in the Holy One of Israel."
  • Scoffers cut off (29:20): "the ruthless shall come to nothing and the scoffer cease, and all who watch to do evil shall be cut off."
  • Courtroom crooks (29:21): those "who by a word make a man out to be an offender, and lay a snare for him who reproves in the gate." Verbal traps, rigged justice.
  • No more shame (29:22): "the LORD, who redeemed Abraham... Jacob shall no more be ashamed, no more shall his face grow pale."
  • Children of his hands (29:23): "when he sees his children, the work of my hands, in his midst, they will sanctify my name... and will stand in awe of the God of Israel."
  • Murmurers taught (29:24): "those who go astray in spirit will come to understanding, and those who murmur will accept instruction." Even the grumblers learn.

Interpretation

The third woe: practical atheism

You turn things upside down! Shall the potter be regarded as the clay, that the thing made should say of its maker, "He did not make me"; or the thing formed say of him who formed it, "He has no understanding"? Isaiah 29:16, ESV

The woe of 29:15 is aimed at planners, probably the palace strategists already whispering about an Egyptian alliance (chapter 30 will drag it into the open). Their sin is not planning; it is planning in the dark, "hiding deep from the LORD their counsel," and the question that gives them away: "Who sees us?" Call it what it is: practical atheism. Nobody in Jerusalem denied God's existence on paper; they denied it in committee, by acting as if he could not see the agenda. Isaiah answers with a picture a child can grasp: a lump of clay looking up from the wheel and telling the potter, "He did not make me; he has no understanding." To hide plans from God you must believe both absurdities at once, that your maker is not really your maker and that he is not very bright. The figure runs through Scripture: Jeremiah watches a potter rework a spoiled vessel and hears, "can I not do with you as this potter has done?" (Jeremiah 18:1-6), and Paul quotes this very verse against the creature who talks back: "Will what is molded say to its molder, 'Why have you made me like this?'" (Romans 9:20-21). Be careful what the image does and does not forbid. Isaiah's God welcomes honest questions; the psalms are full of them, and 30:18 will show him longing to be gracious to askers. What the clay does not get to do is re-assign the roles, grade the potter's competence, or run the wheel. Upside down is the oldest posture in the book: "you felt secure in your wickedness; you said, 'No one sees me'" (47:10). Right side up begins with one admission: he made me, and he understands.

Yet a very little while: the great reversal

Verse 17 pivots on a clock: "Is it not yet a very little while...?" Then a cascade of reversals, and every one of them answers last lesson point for point. The deaf "hear the words of a book" (29:18): the very book that was sealed in 29:11, that neither the literate nor the illiterate could read, gets opened, and to ears that could not hear it. Blind eyes "see out of their gloom" (29:18): the stupor God himself poured out in 29:10 gets lifted by the same hand. The meek and the poor "obtain fresh joy in the LORD" (29:19), while the ruthless, the scoffer (you will recognize the scoffing rulers of 28:14), and the courtroom manipulators of 29:21 are cut off. Notice who does all the verbs. Nobody in this catalog un-seals his own book or performs his own eye surgery; judicial blindness was God's sentence, and only God can reverse it. That is how grace works on the far side of judgment: not the sinner finally trying harder, but "the coming of the LORD" changing what people can see and hear. Jesus stands this text on its feet when he answers John the Baptist: "the blind receive their sight... the deaf hear... and the poor have good news preached to them" (Matthew 11:5). Lebanon's proud forest turned to farmland (29:17) is the same motion in landscape form: what towered gets leveled, what was level gets fruitful, the recurring shape of the whole book (2:12-17).

Jacob no more ashamed

The closing paragraph (29:22-24) is spoken by "the LORD, who redeemed Abraham," a title worth pausing over: Abraham himself was clay from an idol-making family (Joshua 24:2-3) whom God formed into a father of faith. The God who redeemed the first patriarch keeps redeeming his family: "Jacob shall no more be ashamed, no more shall his face grow pale" (29:22). And what replaces the shame? Awe. Jacob sees "his children, the work of my hands," and together they "sanctify my name... and stand in awe of the God of Israel" (29:23). There is the potter image again, redeemed: the same hands the clay insulted in verse 16 turn out to be the hands that made the children of verse 23. Right side up at last: creatures hallowing their maker's name instead of grading his understanding. Verse 24 may be the tenderest line in the chapter: "those who go astray in spirit will come to understanding, and those who murmur will accept instruction." Even the murmurers, the grumblers who fought every lesson, get taught; stubbornness is not terminal when God does the teaching. On our postmil home lens, the "very little while" reversal reads as the gospel age itself: the book opened, eyes opened, the meek inheriting joy in widening circles until the Spirit is poured out and the wilderness becomes a fruitful field (32:15). The MacArthur Study Bible notes (your copy) place the reversal in the future millennium after Israel's national turning. Both lenses agree on the direction of the verbs: God un-seals, God un-blinds, God un-shames, and the end of the story is a family standing in awe.

Application

  • About God: he sees the plans hidden deepest (29:15), and he can open eyes he once let close (29:18). Nothing about you is outside either his sight or his surgery.
  • About you: find your own upside-down turn. Which decision have you been making as if God had no say, the "Who sees us?" corner of your life: a purchase, a relationship, a quiet exit plan? That corner is where the clay is currently lecturing the potter.
  • Do: drag one hidden plan into the light this week. Tell God plainly in prayer what you have been planning and why, then tell one trusted believer. Secrecy was the sin; stated plans can be weighed, corrected, and blessed.
