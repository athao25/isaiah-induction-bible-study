The Big Picture of Isaiah

The man and the moment

Isaiah son of Amoz prophesied to Judah and Jerusalem for roughly sixty years, ~740-680 BC, under four kings: Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, Hezekiah (Isa 1:1). Picture the moment: the superpower Assyria is swallowing nations. In 722 BC it destroys the northern kingdom of Israel. Tiny Judah is next in line, and its kings keep reaching for political alliances (Assyria itself, then Egypt) instead of trusting God. Into that fear Isaiah speaks the book's constant demand: trust the Holy One of Israel, not the nations.

Did one Isaiah write it all?

Your study tools will eventually mention the theory that chapters 40-66 were written centuries later by a "Second Isaiah", mainly because chapter 44-45 names Cyrus, Persia's king, about 150 years before he lived. Notice what drives that theory: the assumption that detailed predictive prophecy cannot happen. But that assumption is precisely what Isaiah 46:9-10 stakes God's deity on: "declaring the end from the beginning". And the New Testament settles it for the Christian: John 12:38-41 quotes Isaiah 53 and Isaiah 6 side by side and says Isaiah said both. One prophet, one book, one sovereign God who tells the future because he ordains it.

The book's center of gravity

Isaiah is quoted in the New Testament more than any prophet, and the reason is its portrait of the coming one, drawn in three overlapping figures:

  • The King (ch. 7-12): a virgin-born child (7:14) called "Mighty God" (9:6), from David's line (11:1).
  • The Servant (ch. 42-53): who suffers in the place of sinners, "pierced for our transgressions" (53:5).
  • The Anointed Conqueror (ch. 59-63): who preaches good news to the poor (61:1) and judges evil.
  • One person fulfills all three: Jesus of Nazareth (Luke 4:17-21, Acts 8:32-35). Isaiah has been called "the fifth Gospel" for good reason.
Your teachers, one map, three lenses
All three of your chosen teachers read Isaiah as inerrant Scripture climaxing in Christ. They part ways on eschatology: MacArthur reads the kingdom pictures (wolf with lamb, 11:6) as a future millennium; White reads them as the present reign of Christ consummated in eternity; Durbin, our home lens, reads them as the gospel's advance in this age. You will meet this fork concretely at chapter 2, and lessons there will argue from the text, not from labels. See the lens card on the book map.
