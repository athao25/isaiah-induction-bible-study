Lesson 0005 · Unit 1: Chapters 1-6

Isaiah 2:1-5: The Mountain of the House of the LORD

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 2:1-5 (ESV, read first!) · First eschatology fork passage

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 2:1-5 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. Five verses, but they carry the weight of the book's whole hope.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • A fresh heading (v. 1): "The word that Isaiah the son of Amoz saw." A new unit begins; after the furnace of chapter 1, a vision of glory.
  • The time stamp (v. 2): "in the latter days." Mark it; the whole interpretive question hangs on when those days are.
  • The impossible geography (v. 2): the mountain of the LORD's house "established as the highest of the mountains." Zion is a modest hill, lower than the Mount of Olives beside it. Isaiah is saying something about supremacy, and he is saying it in mountain language.
  • The direction of the flow (v. 2): "all the nations shall flow to it." Rivers run downhill; here the nations stream uphill, to the highest point. Grace reverses gravity.
  • Willing pilgrims (v. 3): "Come, let us go up... that he may teach us his ways." The nations are not dragged; they exhort each other. What draws them is teaching: "the law", "the word of the LORD."
  • The word's origin (v. 3): "out of Zion shall go forth the law, and the word of the LORD from Jerusalem." The traffic is two-way: nations stream in, the word streams out.
  • The Judge of nations (v. 4): "He shall judge between the nations"; the result is swords beaten to plowshares, spears to pruning hooks, war unlearned.
  • The punchline (v. 5): "O house of Jacob, come, let us walk in the light of the LORD." The vision ends in a present-tense summons to Judah: if the nations will one day walk in his light, why are you not walking in it now?
  • Cross-reference: Micah 4:1-3 carries this oracle almost word for word. Two contemporary prophets, one Spirit-given hope.

Interpretation

When are the latter days?

"It shall come to pass in the latter days that the mountain of the house of the LORD shall be established as the highest of the mountains... and all the nations shall flow to it." Isaiah 2:2, ESV

Start with how the New Testament uses the phrase. Peter, explaining Pentecost, quotes Joel: "in the last days... I will pour out my Spirit" and says "this is that" (Acts 2:16-17). Hebrews opens: "in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son" (Heb 1:2). In apostolic usage, the latter days began with the coming of Christ. And notice what actually happened from Jerusalem: the word of the LORD went out from Zion, literally, at Pentecost and through the book of Acts (Luke 24:47, "beginning from Jerusalem"). Hebrews 12:22 tells the church, present tense: "you have come to Mount Zion." The nations streaming to the mountain to be taught God's ways is a portrait of the discipling of the nations (Matt 28:19), which has been underway for twenty centuries.

Fulfillment: a mustard seed, not a light switch

Our home lens (postmillennial, as Durbin argues) reads verses 2-4 as describing this gospel age in its growth and its goal: the kingdom spreads like mustard seed and leaven (Matt 13:31-33), gradually, until the earth is full of the knowledge of the LORD. On this reading, verse 4's beaten swords are the trajectory of the gospel's triumph in history, before Christ returns. The vision is not a photograph of the present; it is the direction history is being pulled by the reigning Christ (1 Cor 15:25).

The fork: three Reformed readings of the same mountain
MacArthur (premillennial): this is the future millennial kingdom; a restored, literal Jerusalem becomes earth's capital after Christ returns, and v. 4's peace is enforced by the present King. Strength: takes v. 4's universal peace at full face value; honest readers must admit the nations have not yet unlearned war. White (amillennial): fulfilled spiritually now in the church, where the nations truly stream to Zion (Heb 12:22), and consummated only in the eternal state; no golden age within history. Durbin (postmillennial, our home lens): same starting point as White (the latter days are now, the mountain is the church), but the fulfillment is progressive and public: the nations will be discipled in history, and v. 4 describes where the gospel is taking the world before the end. The debate is real and in-house; all three agree on the destination: Christ exalted, nations gathered, war abolished.

Why mountain language?

In the ancient world every nation set its gods on its high places. Isaiah's claim is that the LORD's hill, physically unimpressive, will tower over every rival worship on earth: supremacy, not topography. The true God will be publicly first, and the nations will know where to go to learn his ways.

Application

  • Mission optimism: pray and give toward missions like someone who believes the nations actually will come. This text forbids gospel pessimism; the mountain will be raised whether the news cycle cooperates or not.
  • Walk now (v. 5): Isaiah's own application move is the model. Future glory is not for spectating; "come, let us walk in the light of the LORD" today, in the ordinary obediences of this week.
  • Check your magnetism: the nations are drawn by God's word taught. Churches and Christians attract the same way; not by cleverness, but by the word going forth.
