Lesson 0020 · Unit 2: Chapters 7-12

Isaiah 11:1-10: The Shoot from the Stump

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 11:1-10 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 11:1-10 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were asked to watch what grows from the stump of Jesse, what rests on him, and how far the knowledge of the LORD spreads. Have your answers ready.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • A shoot from a stump (v. 1): "There shall come forth a shoot from the stump of Jesse, and a branch from his roots shall bear fruit." Not from David the king but from Jesse the farmer: the royal tree cut down to its lowly root, yet alive.
  • The Spirit rests (v. 2): "the Spirit of the LORD shall rest upon him", then three paired gifts: wisdom and understanding, counsel and might, knowledge and the fear of the LORD. Count them with the first line: a sevenfold fullness.
  • He judges blind (vv. 3-4): "He shall not judge by what his eyes see, or decide disputes by what his ears hear, but with righteousness he shall judge the poor." No bribes, no spin, no optics.
  • The breath that kills (v. 4): "he shall strike the earth with the rod of his mouth, and with the breath of his lips he shall kill the wicked." His weapon is his word.
  • The belt (v. 5): "Righteousness shall be the belt of his waist, and faithfulness the belt of his loins." What he is holds everything else together.
  • Predators and prey at peace (vv. 6-8): wolf with lamb, leopard with goat, calf with lion, "and a little child shall lead them"; the nursing child plays over the cobra's hole.
  • The reason for the peace (v. 9): "They shall not hurt or destroy in all my holy mountain; for the earth shall be full of the knowledge of the LORD as the waters cover the sea." Mark that "for": the peace has a cause.
  • A signal for the nations (v. 10): "In that day the root of Jesse, who shall stand as a signal for the peoples, of him shall the nations inquire, and his resting place shall be glorious."

Interpretation

Two trees, one pivot

Do not read chapter 11 without the last two verses of chapter 10 in your ear. There, "the majestic forest will fall by the Majestic One": Assyria felled, stump dead forever. Here, another tree has already been cut down to a stump: the house of David, reduced by judgment and exile to the family root it sprang from, plain Jesse of Bethlehem. But this stump is not dead. "There shall come forth a shoot": tender, green, alive. Assyria's greatness ends at the axe; David's line begins again after it. This is typology in miniature: God's pattern of bringing life out of what looks finished. The New Testament hears this verse everywhere: Revelation 5:5 names Jesus "the Root of David", and Matthew 2:23 plays on the Hebrew word for shoot, netzer, when the Messiah settles in Nazareth. The Messiah is the shoot.

The Spirit-carried King

Verse 2 gives the shoot his equipment, and it is not armies or revenue: "the Spirit of the LORD shall rest upon him." Rest, not visit. Judges and kings in the Old Testament had the Spirit come upon them for a task; on this King the Spirit settles and stays, which is exactly what John the Baptist was told to look for: "he on whom you see the Spirit descend and remain" (John 1:33). The three pairs (wisdom and understanding, counsel and might, knowledge and the fear of the LORD) cover the whole job of ruling: seeing rightly, deciding rightly, and revering rightly. Verses 3-5 show the result: a judge who cannot be fooled by appearances, who rules for the poor and meek, whose word itself is the weapon (Paul borrows "the breath of his lips" for the defeat of the lawless one in 2 Thessalonians 2:8), and who is belted, held together, by righteousness and faithfulness. Ahaz judged by what his eyes saw (two smoldering firebrands looked terrifying, ch. 7). This King never will.

The peaceable kingdom, and the fork in the road

They shall not hurt or destroy in all my holy mountain; for the earth shall be full of the knowledge of the LORD as the waters cover the sea. Isaiah 11:9, ESV

Wolf and lamb, leopard and goat, a toddler leading them: what is this describing, and when? This is the second great eschatology fork of the book (the first was the mountain of 2:1-5, lesson 5), and the three teachers you know read it three ways.

  • MacArthur (premillennial): this is a literal description of the future millennial earth after Christ returns; the curse is partially lifted, animal natures actually change, Israel is restored under her King. See his notes on chapter 11 in your MacArthur Study Bible notes. Strength: it takes the concrete details seriously and refuses to let them evaporate into decoration.
  • White (amillennial): the animal peace is prophetic imagery for the new creation at Christ's return, and in the present age it pictures the reconciling power of the gospel in the church, where natural enemies sit at one table. Strength: it honors the poetic register; Isaiah paints, he does not draft zoology.
  • Durbin (postmillennial, our home lens): watch the logic of verse 9. The peace does not fall from the sky; it spreads. "They shall not hurt or destroy... for the earth shall be full of the knowledge of the LORD." Peace advances as knowledge advances, and knowledge advances the way water does: gradually, unstoppably, covering everything. Verse 10 says the nations inquire of the root of Jesse, and Paul in Romans 15:12 quotes it in the present tense of his own Gentile mission: "in him will the Gentiles hope." If the nations are already seeking the signal, the filling of the earth is already underway, culminating in a renewed creation where even the predation of verses 6-8 is finally healed.

Be honest about the tension: the premillennial reading asks fairly, "why describe animals if you mean ideas?", and the postmillennial reading answers fairly, "why give the peace a cause (knowledge filling the earth) if it arrives all at once?" Hold your lens with conviction and your brothers with charity. All three readings agree on the center: the shoot of Jesse reigns, his knowledge will fill the earth, and the end of the story is peace.

Application

  • About God: the Spirit-equipped King "shall not judge by what his eyes see" (11:3). You have never once been misjudged by Jesus. Every other court, including the court of your own self-assessment, works from appearances; his does not.
  • About you: where do you judge by what your eyes see and your ears hear: assessing people by optics, first impressions, reputation? Name one person you have filed away on thin evidence, and this week reopen the file.
  • Do: begin memory verse 5 today (card below), and pray verse 9 for one specific unreached place or unbelieving person: that the knowledge of the LORD would cover them like water.
