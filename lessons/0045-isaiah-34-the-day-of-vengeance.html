Lesson 0045 · Unit 5: Chapters 28-35

Isaiah 34: The Day of Vengeance

~25 min · Passage: Isaiah 34:1-17 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 34 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. This is a hard chapter, and you were sent into it with three things to watch: a sword that drinks its fill in the heavens, the fate of Edom, and skies rolled up like a scroll (34:4). Keep all three marked; each one matters today.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • The whole world subpoenaed (34:1): "Draw near, O nations, to hear... Let the earth hear, and all that fills it." Nobody is excused from this courtroom.
  • Devoted to destruction (34:2): "the LORD is enraged against all the nations... he has devoted them to destruction." Herem, the old holy-war word from Joshua.
  • Unburied dead (34:3): stench rising from corpses, "the mountains shall flow with their blood." Isaiah does not let you look away.
  • The sky comes down (34:4): "All the host of heaven shall rot away, and the skies roll up like a scroll," stars dropping like fig leaves.
  • A sword in the heavens first (34:5): "my sword has drunk its fill in the heavens; behold, it descends for judgment upon Edom."
  • A sacrifice in Bozrah (34:6): the slaughter is described as sacrificial, "a great slaughter in the land of Edom," with lambs, goats, and rams.
  • Soaked land (34:7): "their land shall drink its fill of blood, and their soil shall be gorged with fat."
  • The day named (34:8): "the LORD has a day of vengeance, a year of recompense for the cause of Zion." The anchor verse of the chapter.
  • Sodom weather (34:9-10): streams turned to pitch, soil to sulfur, burning "night and day... its smoke shall go up forever."
  • New tenants (34:11): hawk and porcupine, owl and raven take possession of the ruins.
  • Surveyors of nothing (34:11): God stretches over Edom "the line of confusion and the plumb line of emptiness," the Hebrew words tohu and bohu from Genesis 1:2.
  • Palace wildlife (34:13-15): thorns in the fortresses, jackals, ostriches, wild animals meeting, the night bird settling and finding rest.
  • Check the record (34:16-17): "Seek and read from the book of the LORD: Not one of these shall be missing." He casts the lot; the creatures possess it forever.

Interpretation

Two futures, one page apart

Before anything else, see where you are standing. Chapters 34 and 35 are a matched pair, a diptych closing Unit 5: two panels hung side by side, painted with the same brush. In chapter 34 a living land becomes a desert by judgment; in chapter 35 a desert becomes a garden by grace, and the two chapters share vocabulary on purpose (streams, haunts of jackals, the ransomed). All six woes of this unit have been putting one question to Judah's leaders: whom will you trust? Chapter 34 and chapter 35 are the two places that question finally leads. Every reader of this unit has been choosing between these panels all along; today you look hard at the dark one so that next lesson the bright one lands with its full weight.

The cosmic courtroom

For the LORD has a day of vengeance, a year of recompense for the cause of Zion. Isaiah 34:8, ESV

The chapter opens with a summons wider than any oracle in Unit 3: all nations, the earth, the world, "all that comes from it" (34:1). Then a strange sequence: before the sword touches Edom, it "has drunk its fill in the heavens" (34:5). Judgment begins above before it lands below; "the host of heaven" rots away (34:4) as well as the armies of earth. You have seen this before: behind the nations stand powers, and on the day of the LORD the sword goes through both, just as Leviathan fell before the vineyard bloomed (27:1). Revelation reaches for this very chapter when the sixth seal opens: "the sky vanished like a scroll that is being rolled up" and the stars fell like figs (Revelation 6:13-14). Now weigh the anchor verse. "Vengeance" in Scripture is not temper; it is docketed justice, a day and a year on the calendar of a Judge who has recorded every wrong done against his people, "recompense for the cause of Zion" (34:8). Isaiah will return to this exact ledger at 61:2 ("the day of vengeance of our God") and at 63:1-6, where a figure comes striding from Bozrah with stained garments. And why Edom as the representative defendant? Because Edom is the perpetual brother-enemy: Esau's line, family to Jacob, who cheered from the sidelines when Jerusalem fell ("Lay it bare, lay it bare, down to its foundations!", Psalm 137:7; see Obadiah 10-14). Hatred of Zion from a brother's house is the type of all the world's hatred of Zion; judge that, and you have judged it all.

Uncreation: Genesis run backwards

Verses 9-10 borrow Sodom's file: pitch, sulfur, smoke going up, night and day unquenched (Genesis 19:24-28). John borrows the same line for final judgment: "the smoke of their torment goes up forever and ever" (Revelation 14:11), and over fallen Babylon, "her smoke goes up forever and ever" (Revelation 19:3). Then comes the strangest image in the chapter: God as surveyor, stretching over Edom "the line of confusion and the plumb line of emptiness" (34:11). Builders stretch lines to raise walls; God stretches these to lower them. The two Hebrew words are tohu and bohu, "without form and void," lifted straight from Genesis 1:2. The verdict is formal: Edom is measured back into pre-creation chaos, judgment as creation run backwards, the same move that broke the world-city into rubble in 24:10 (where tohu appears too). Yet look closely at the wasteland: the owl has a nest, the jackals a haunt, every creature a mate, and God himself "has cast the lot for them" and "portioned it out to them with the line" (34:17). Even his unmaking is orderly. Judgment is never chaos escaping God's hands; it is chaos assigned, surveyed, and leased out on his terms.

Seek and read from the book

Seek and read from the book of the LORD: Not one of these shall be missing; none shall be without her mate. For the mouth of the LORD has commanded, and his Spirit has gathered them. Isaiah 34:16, ESV

Isaiah ends the chapter the way he ended the oracles of Unit 3: with a dare. File this prophecy, he says, then come back later, owl by owl, and check that "not one of these shall be missing" (34:16). This prophet signs his work, like Moab's three years and Tyre's seventy. And the book already grades out: Edom was broken by Babylon, squeezed out by the Nabatean Arabs, and by New Testament times had vanished as a nation; travelers through that region for centuries have described exactly the silent, creature-haunted ruin of verses 11-15. Now the pastoral word, because you may be asking why a chapter like this is in your Bible at all. Answer: because a God with no day of vengeance would be a God indifferent to the cause of Zion, a judge who shrugs at what was done to his people. "Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord" (Romans 12:19) is comfort, not menace, for those hidden in him. The MacArthur Study Bible notes (your copy) read the chapter as the future tribulation judgments before the millennium; on any lens, hold on to this: the same fire that ends Edom in chapter 34 lights the highway home in chapter 35. God unmakes the wasteland-makers so that the wasteland can bloom.

Application

  • About God: his patience has a docket. "A day of vengeance, a year of recompense" (34:8) means no wrong against his people is forgotten, only scheduled. Vengeance belongs to him, which is exactly why it does not belong to you (Romans 12:19).
  • About you: locate any place you are quietly playing Edom, a spectator cheering someone's downfall from the wall, even someone who may deserve it. And be honest: do chapters like this embarrass you into editing God? Let the text discipline your instincts, not the reverse.
  • Do: before the next lesson, read Isaiah 34 and 35 back to back in one sitting, out loud. Then write one sentence on what changes when you hear them as a pair rather than alone.
