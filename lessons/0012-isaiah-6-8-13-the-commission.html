Isaiah 6:8-13: Here Am I, Send Me... to Harden

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 6:8-13 (ESV, read first!) · Unit 1 closes today

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 6:8-13 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. The lesson checks your work; it cannot check work that does not exist.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • The order of events (v. 8): "Whom shall I send?" comes only after the coal touches Isaiah's lips (vv. 6-7). Cleansing precedes commission. God does not ask for volunteers until guilt is atoned for.
  • Isaiah volunteers (v. 8): "Here I am! Send me." No arm-twisting, no Jonah-style flight. The man who said "Woe is me" now runs toward the mission. Forgiveness produces eagerness.
  • The shocking job description (vv. 9-10): the preaching itself will "make the heart of this people dull", their ears heavy, their eyes blind, "lest they... turn and be healed". Underline that; interpretation must wrestle with it.
  • Isaiah's question (v. 11): not "why, Lord?" but "how long, O Lord?" He accepts the mission and asks about its duration.
  • The answer (vv. 11-12): until cities are ruined, houses empty, the land a desolation, the people removed far away. This is exile language.
  • Repeated burning (v. 13): even the surviving tenth is "burned again", like a felled terebinth or oak. Judgment upon judgment.
  • The last five words (v. 13): "The holy seed is its stump." After all the burning, a living stump remains. The chapter refuses to end in ash.

Interpretation

Does God really send preaching in order to harden?

Yes, and the text will not let us soften it: "Make the heart of this people dull" (v. 10). Here is how to hold it rightly. Judah has already had decades of prophets, and chapters 1-5 showed a people who "call evil good and good evil" (5:20). When truth is persistently rejected, the same word that would have melted a soft heart bakes a hard one, the way one sun melts wax and hardens clay. God now ordains Isaiah's faithful preaching as an instrument of that judgment: not withholding truth, but giving truth to people whose rejection of it will confirm them in unbelief. Romans 1 runs on the same logic: "God gave them up" to what they had chosen (Rom 1:24, 26, 28). This is judicial hardening: God's sovereignty extends even over unbelief, yet human responsibility remains fully intact, for no one in Judah was hardened against their will to repent. They loved the darkness first; the hardening is sentence, not entrapment.

Why does the New Testament quote this so often?

Isaiah 6:9-10 is among the most-quoted Old Testament passages in the New. Jesus cites it to explain why he teaches in parables to a rejecting generation (Matt 13:14-15). John cites it to explain why Israel did not believe despite the signs, and adds the stunning note that Isaiah "saw his glory and spoke of him", identifying the enthroned King of Isaiah 6 as Christ (John 12:39-41). Paul closes Acts with it, turning to the Gentiles when the synagogue will not hear (Acts 28:26-27). The apostles reached for this text whenever they needed to explain the mystery that the Messiah's own people refused him: Isaiah had already told them it would be so.

How long? And what is the stump?

The answer to "how long" is: until the land lies empty (vv. 11-12), fulfilled when Babylon carried Judah into exile in 586 BC. Even the tenth that survives is burned again (the waves of invasion and deportation). But the final line reverses everything: "the holy seed is its stump." The remnant hope of the whole book compressed into five words. A felled tree is not a dead tree; the life is in the stump. Hold the image: in 11:1 "there shall come forth a shoot from the stump of Jesse", and the shoot is the Messiah. The holy seed line runs through the burning, down the centuries, to Christ.

Application

  • About God: he is sovereign even over rejection. No unbelief surprises him or derails his plan; the hardening of many and the saving of a remnant are both inside his purpose.
  • About faithfulness: Isaiah preached a lifetime with orders that guaranteed mostly deaf ears. Success in God's service is measured by obedience, not visible results. If you serve, teach, parent, or witness with little fruit to show, Isaiah 6 is your commission too.
  • Pray: the passage should drive you to ask for a soft heart. "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts" (Heb 3:15). Ask God to keep his word melting you, not baking you.
