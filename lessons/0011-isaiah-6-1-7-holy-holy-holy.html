Lesson 0011 · Unit 1: Chapters 1-6

Isaiah 6:1-7: The King on the Throne

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 6:1-7 (ESV, read first!) · Memory verse 2 activates today

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 6:1-7 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. This is the vision that governs the entire book; give it your slowest reading yet.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • The date marker (v. 1): "In the year that King Uzziah died". Uzziah reigned about fifty-two years, most of them strong and stable (2 Chr 26). His death (~740 BC) ends the only era most of Judah had ever known, with Assyria rising. The earthly throne empties; Isaiah sees the heavenly one occupied.
  • The posture (v. 1): "sitting upon a throne, high and lifted up". Not pacing, not anxious. Seated: the posture of settled reign.
  • The robe (v. 1): its hem alone fills the temple. Isaiah describes the edges; the center is beyond him.
  • The seraphim (v. 2): "burning ones", each with six wings, and four of the six are used for covering: faces (even sinless creatures do not gaze) and feet. Two for flying, four for reverence.
  • The threefold cry (v. 3): "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts; the whole earth is full of his glory!" One attribute, tripled.
  • The shaking and smoke (v. 4): foundations tremble at the voices; the house fills with smoke. Sinai's phenomena (Exod 19:18) relocated to the temple.
  • The prophet's cry (v. 5): "Woe is me! For I am lost... a man of unclean lips". Isaiah pronounced six woes on others in chapter 5; the seventh falls on himself.
  • Lips, specifically (vv. 5-7): the confessed uncleanness is of the lips, and the coal touches the lips. The remedy lands exactly on the wound.
  • The coal's source (v. 6): taken "from the altar", the place of sacrifice.
  • The verdict (v. 7): "your guilt is taken away, and your sin atoned for". Isaiah does nothing in this scene but confess; heaven does the rest.

Interpretation

"Holy, holy, holy": the tripled attribute

And one called to another and said: "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts; the whole earth is full of his glory!" Isaiah 6:3, ESV

Hebrew makes superlatives by repetition: "gold, gold" means purest gold (2 Kgs 25:15, literally). Triple repetition is the language's strongest possible emphasis, and Scripture applies it to exactly one divine attribute. Not "love, love, love" or "wrath, wrath, wrath", but "holy, holy, holy", here and in Revelation 4:8. Qadosh means set apart: morally pure, yes, but deeper still, transcendently other, in a category no creature shares. Holiness is not one attribute among many; it is the Godness of God, the blazing otherness in which all his attributes burn. And note the second line: this transcendent one is not remote. "The whole earth is full of his glory": the seraphim see what chapter 5's darkness hid.

Why "woe is me" and not "wow"

Isaiah is the best man in Judah, a prophet already preaching. One sight of the throne and he pronounces a funeral cry over himself: "I am lost" (literally, unraveled, coming apart). Proximity to holiness does not flatter; it exposes. And his conscience strikes precisely at his instrument: "unclean lips", the preacher's own tool. This is the Bible's consistent pattern: Job repents in dust (Job 42:5-6), Peter begs "depart from me" (Luke 5:8), John falls as though dead (Rev 1:17). Any theology, or worship style, that produces casualness before God has not yet seen what Isaiah saw. R. C. Sproul built his classic The Holiness of God on this chapter for exactly that reason.

The coal from the altar: atonement before assignment

The seraph's coal is not generic fire; it comes "from the altar", where sacrificial blood was shed and sin was dealt with by substitution. The logic of the Levitical altar (a life given in place of the guilty, Lev 17:11) is pressed, burning, against the confessed point of uncleanness, and heaven pronounces: "your guilt is taken away, and your sin atoned for" (v. 7). Isaiah contributes confession; God supplies cleansing. Grace initiates, grace applies, grace declares. The altar's coal is a shadow of the true sacrifice: the Servant of chapter 53, whose wounds heal unclean people. Remember lesson 3's held question, how scarlet becomes snow: here the answer's shape appears, atonement from God's own altar.

Whose glory did Isaiah see?

John answers directly. After quoting Isaiah 6:10 and Isaiah 53:1 together, he writes: "Isaiah said these things because he saw his glory and spoke of him", and in context "him" is Jesus (John 12:37-41). The enthroned King of Isaiah's vision is the preincarnate Son. James White presses this text constantly in defense of the deity of Christ, and it is fair pressing: the LORD of hosts whom seraphim cannot look upon is the same Lord who took flesh, was despised, and bore our griefs. Keep John 12:41 pinned to this chapter; it turns a majestic vision into a christological one.

Application

  • The order of true worship: sight of holiness, then conviction, then atonement received, and only then (next lesson) service offered. Every reversal of that order produces either casual worship or anxious service. Where does your own devotional life start on that sequence?
  • About assurance: v. 7 is a declaration, not a negotiation. If you are in Christ, the true altar has spoken over you what the coal spoke over Isaiah: guilt taken, sin atoned. Preach that to your conscience in the exact moments it unravels.
  • About your lips: Isaiah's conviction landed on his speech. Let today's prayer be specific: ask God to cleanse what your lips said this week, then to use them.
