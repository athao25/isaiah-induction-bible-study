Lesson 0049 · Unit 6: Chapters 36-39

Isaiah 38: Hezekiah's Fifteen Years

~25 min · Passage: Isaiah 38:1-22 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 38 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were told the clock runs backwards here, since chapters 38-39 happen before the siege of 36-37, and you were sent to watch three things: the opening verdict, Hezekiah's response, and the shadow sign. Keep all three marked.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • The verdict (38:1): "Set your house in order, for you shall die, you shall not recover." Isaiah delivers it to a sick king with no cushioning.
  • The wall (38:2): "Then Hezekiah turned his face to the wall and prayed to the LORD." No court, no audience; just a man, a wall, and God.
  • Tears as argument (38:3): "Remember, O LORD, how I have walked before you in faithfulness... And Hezekiah wept bitterly."
  • The answer (38:5): "I have heard your prayer; I have seen your tears; behold, I will add fifteen years to your life." Tears seen, not just words heard.
  • Two promises in one breath (38:6): "I will deliver you and this city out of the hand of the king of Assyria." The city is still under threat, which dates this scene before chapters 36-37.
  • The sign (38:8): "I will make the shadow cast by the declining sun on the dial of Ahaz turn back ten steps." The sun's shadow runs in reverse.
  • A king's psalm (38:9): "A writing of Hezekiah king of Judah, after he had been sick and had recovered." He wrote it down from the far side.
  • The dread (38:10-11): "In the middle of my days I must depart... I shall not see the LORD in the land of the living."
  • Tent and loom (38:12): "my dwelling is plucked up and removed from me like a shepherd's tent; like a weaver I have rolled up my life; he cuts me off from the loom."
  • Lion and birds (38:13-14): "like a lion he breaks all my bones... Like a swallow or a crane I chirp; I moan like a dove... O Lord, I am oppressed; be my pledge of safety!"
  • The turn (38:17): "Behold, it was for my welfare that I had great bitterness... you have cast all my sins behind your back."
  • The living thank you (38:18-19): "Sheol does not thank you... The living, the living, he thanks you, as I do this day; the father makes known to the children your faithfulness."
  • Music for life (38:20): "The LORD will save me, and we will play my music on stringed instruments all the days of our lives, at the house of the LORD."
  • A cake of figs (38:21): "Let them take a cake of figs and apply it to the boil, that he may recover." A prescription inside a miracle.
  • The last question (38:22): "What is the sign that I shall go up to the house of the LORD?" His hope aims at the temple, not just at survival.

Interpretation

Mortality without anesthesia

"Set your house in order, for you shall die" (38:1). Read that slowly, because it is not a special sentence passed on Hezekiah; it is the standing sentence on every mortal, and Hezekiah merely received a date. Scripture elsewhere asks for exactly this arithmetic: "teach us to number our days" (Psalm 90:12). What the chapter teaches is what a believer does with the sentence. Hezekiah does not bargain with Isaiah, summon physicians first, or issue a royal protest; he turns his face to the wall, away from every earthly resource, and prays (38:2). And he weeps. Notice what God says he noticed: "I have heard your prayer; I have seen your tears" (38:5). Tears are part of prayer's vocabulary; God reads them. As for Hezekiah's plea, "remember how I have walked before you in faithfulness" (38:3), do not misread it as works-boasting. This is covenant idiom, the appeal of a son reminding his father of a real relationship, the same way David prays in the Psalms. Verse 17 will show that Hezekiah knows exactly what his standing rests on, and it is not his record.

The sundial of Ahaz

The sign God gives is aimed with a marksman's precision: "the shadow cast by the declining sun on the dial of Ahaz" runs backwards ten steps (38:8). You know that name. A generation earlier, at this same royal court, God invited Ahaz to ask for a sign "deep as Sheol or high as heaven," and Ahaz refused with fake piety (7:10-12). Now the monument that carries the unbelieving father's name becomes the stage on which the believing son receives what his father declined. The dial of Ahaz preaches against Ahaz. And consider what the sign says: the shadow of a declining sun is the most relentless thing a dying man can watch; every step it descends is time he does not have. God reverses it. Time itself is soft clay to the Lord of it, and the God who added fifteen years demonstrates the addition on the instrument that measures them. On the chronology, notice that verse 6 binds two promises together: added years and a delivered city. The siege has not happened yet; this scene precedes chapters 36-37, and the book ordered the material by theme rather than by calendar, saving the Babylon-shaped ending of chapter 39 for the hinge into chapters 40 and following.

The psalm from the far side

Behold, it was for my welfare that I had great bitterness; but in love you have delivered my life from the pit of destruction, for you have cast all my sins behind your back. Isaiah 38:17, ESV

Hezekiah wrote his sickness up afterwards (38:9), and the psalm's movement is the whole lesson. First, honest dread, in some of the sharpest images of mortality in Scripture: life as a shepherd's tent plucked up overnight, as a weaver's cloth cut off the loom mid-pattern (38:12), God as a lion breaking bones (38:13), the king reduced to bird noises, chirping like a swallow, moaning like a dove (38:14). Scripture does not require the dying to pretend. Then the turn, and it is an audit: "it was for my welfare that I had great bitterness" (38:17). Not despite the bitterness; the bitterness itself, weighed afterwards, lands in the welfare column. Then the ground of it all: "you have cast all my sins behind your back." Forgiveness pictured spatially: God puts your sins where he will not look, behind his own back; Micah says he hurls them "into the depths of the sea" (Micah 7:19). A forgiven man can read even his worst season as mercy. Then the survivor's job description: "the living, the living, he thanks you... the father makes known to the children your faithfulness" (38:19). Recovered life has two duties, gratitude and catechesis; you tell the next generation. Be honest about verse 18's dim view of death ("Sheol does not thank you"): Hezekiah lived before the fuller light. You have already read further than he could see, at 25:8 (death swallowed forever) and 26:19 (your dead shall live), and the full dawn is Christ, who "abolished death and brought life and immortality to light" (2 Timothy 1:10).

Means, miracle, and the sober footnote

Verse 21 deserves more attention than it usually gets: "Let them take a cake of figs and apply it to the boil, that he may recover." The same God who promised fifteen years by prophetic word prescribes a poultice. Scripture holds means and miracle in one hand without embarrassment; using medicine is not unbelief, and prayer is not a substitute for the figs. Take both, thank God for both. Then let the narrator's arithmetic sober you. Inside the fifteen added years fall two events: the Babylon envoys of chapter 39, where Hezekiah's pride opens the door to exile, and the birth of Manasseh, who was twelve when he took the throne (2 Kings 21:1), which puts his birth squarely inside the added years, and Manasseh became Judah's worst king. Extended life is a real gift, and it is not a guarantee of wisdom; what you do with recovered time still matters. The MacArthur Study Bible notes (your copy) walk the timeline details. That is exactly where the next lesson goes.

Memory verse 8 review: Isaiah 30:15
Hezekiah's sickbed is the involuntary version of your memory verse: returning and rest imposed on a king who could do nothing else. Recite 30:15 from memory, then recite 25:8, the verse his psalm was still waiting for: death swallowed forever. Schedule and method: memory verses.

Application

  • About God: he numbers our days (38:1), counts our tears (38:5), and files forgiven sins behind his back where he will not look at them (38:17). All three at once, about the same person.
  • About you: if Isaiah delivered Hezekiah's sentence to you today, what in your house is not in order? Relationally, financially, spiritually. Write the honest list; do not edit it for respectability.
  • Do: put your house in order deliberately this month, without waiting for a diagnosis. Reconcile one relationship. Write or update the practical documents your family would need. And obey 38:19 directly: tell one child or younger believer a specific story of God's faithfulness to you.
