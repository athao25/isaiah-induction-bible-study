Lesson 0014 · Unit 2: Chapters 7-12

Isaiah 7:10-25: The Virgin Shall Conceive

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 7:10-25 (ESV, read first!) · One of the most contested verses in the Bible, handled slowly

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 7:10-25 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. The lesson checks your work; it cannot check work that does not exist.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • An open-ended offer (vv. 10-11): God invites Ahaz to ask for any sign, "deep as Sheol or high as heaven". Unlimited confirmation, free of charge, from the God Ahaz is refusing to trust.
  • The pious dodge (v. 12): "I will not ask, and I will not put the LORD to the test." It sounds like Deuteronomy 6:16; it is actually disobedience dressed as reverence, since God himself commanded the asking.
  • The audience widens (v. 13): "Hear then, O house of David... you weary my God". The "you" shifts to plural; the sign that follows is addressed to the whole dynasty, not to Ahaz alone.
  • The sign itself (v. 14): "the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel", which means God with us.
  • A timeline attached (vv. 15-16): before a certain child knows how to refuse evil and choose good, "the land whose two kings you dread will be deserted". The near threat has an expiry date measured in a childhood.
  • The twist (v. 17): the king Ahaz hired for protection becomes the punishment: "the king of Assyria" ends the verse like a dropped stone.
  • Four "in that day" pictures (vv. 18-25): flies and bees whistled in, a hired razor shaving the land, curds and honey (poverty food), briers and thorns where vineyards stood.

Interpretation

Why is refusing a sign unbelief here?

Testing God is demanding proof on your terms while distrusting him (Massah, Exod 17). But when God commands you to ask, refusing is not humility; it is rebellion with a religious accent. Ahaz refuses because his mind is made up: 2 Kings 16:7-9 records him stripping temple silver to pay Tiglath-pileser of Assyria, saying "I am your servant and your son." He has chosen his savior, and it is not Yahweh. Verse 13 names the offense precisely: wearying God, exhausting his patience with pretend piety over settled unbelief.

Who is the virgin's son?

"Therefore the Lord himself will give you a sign. Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel." Isaiah 7:14, ESV

Handle the famous word honestly. Hebrew almah means a young woman of marriageable age; it is not the clinical term for virgin (betulah), though in the Old Testament almah never refers to a married woman. Centuries before Christ, the Jewish translators of the Greek Septuagint rendered it parthenos, virgin. Matthew, writing under inspiration, declares the verse fulfilled in the virgin conception of Jesus: "they shall call his name Immanuel, which means God with us" (Matt 1:22-23). On this all three of your teachers stand together: MacArthur, White, and Durbin all affirm that 7:14 finds its true fulfillment in the virgin birth.

But what did the sign mean in 735 BC? Verses 15-16 tie a timeline to a child growing up in Ahaz's own day: before such a child can refuse evil and choose good, Syria and Ephraim will be empty threats. The next chapter supplies that near-term clock: Isaiah's son Maher-shalal-hash-baz, whose birth marks the same countdown (8:3-4). So read the sign with two horizons, like a mountain range: a near ridge (a child born soon, proving the prophecy's clock) and the far summit the words themselves demand. For no child of Ahaz's day was born of a virgin or could carry the name "God with us" in fullness; and Isaiah will not let the theme drop: the land belongs to Immanuel (8:8), and the coming child is "Mighty God" (9:6). The dynasty that refused the sign is given a sign beyond itself: from the house of David will come a son who is God with us in person.

The hired razor

Verses 17-25 preach one grim sentence: the savior you buy becomes the sword you feel. Assyria, the "help" Ahaz purchased, is pictured as a razor God himself hires to shave Judah bald (v. 20), a swarm God whistles for like a beekeeper (v. 18). Sovereignty cuts both ways: the empire Ahaz feared into obedience is merely an instrument in the hand of the God he refused. The land of vineyards becomes briers and thorns; the covenant curses of chapter 5 arrive by the road Ahaz himself paved.

Application

  • About God: grace overrules unbelief. Ahaz refuses the sign; God gives one anyway, and a better one than Ahaz could have imagined. Your failures do not cancel God's purposes; they cannot even slow them.
  • About you: watch for pious language covering settled disobedience: "I don't want to bother God with this" while you engineer your own rescue. Name the false savior you are paying tribute to; false saviors always send an invoice.
  • Worship: "Immanuel, God with us" is not a Christmas decoration; it was spoken into a war cabinet meeting for shaking hearts. Take the name into whatever you dread this week: God with us (Matt 28:20).
