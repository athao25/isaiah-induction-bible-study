Lesson 0029 · Unit 3: Chapters 13-23

Isaiah 19:16-20:6: Egypt My People

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 19:16-20:6 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 19:16-25 and chapter 20 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were asked to watch what Egypt is called by the end of chapter 19. If that title did not stop you in your tracks, read verses 24-25 once more, slowly.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • Five "in that day" scenes (19:16, 18, 19, 23, 24): the second half of the oracle is built from five stacked snapshots, each opening with the same phrase.
  • Fear first (19:16-17): Egypt trembles "before the hand that the LORD of hosts shakes over them," and tiny Judah becomes "a terror to the Egyptians."
  • Egypt speaking Hebrew (19:18): "five cities in the land of Egypt... speak the language of Canaan and swear allegiance to the LORD of hosts."
  • An altar in Egypt (19:19-20): "there will be an altar to the LORD in the midst of the land of Egypt," a sign and a witness.
  • Struck to be healed (19:22): "the LORD will strike Egypt, striking and healing, and they will return to the LORD, and he will listen to their pleas for mercy and heal them."
  • A highway (19:23): "a highway from Egypt to Assyria... and the Egyptians will worship with the Assyrians." The two superpowers, joined by a road of worship.
  • The unthinkable blessing (19:24-25): "Blessed be Egypt my people, and Assyria the work of my hands, and Israel my inheritance."
  • A named date (20:1): "the year that the commander in chief, who was sent by Sargon the king of Assyria, came to Ashdod and fought against it."
  • The prophet stripped (20:2-4): Isaiah walks "naked and barefoot" for three years, a living sign of how Assyria will lead away Egyptian and Cushite captives.
  • The punchline question (20:5-6): "we, how shall we escape?", spoken by everyone who had trusted in Egypt.

Interpretation

Struck in order to be healed

And the LORD will strike Egypt, striking and healing, and they will return to the LORD, and he will listen to their pleas for mercy and heal them. Isaiah 19:22, ESV

Last lesson ended in rubble; this lesson explains what the rubble was for. Verse 22 gives the whole oracle its logic in one line: "striking and healing." The demolition of 19:1-15 was surgery, not execution. And watch the conversion language pile up: Egypt cries to the LORD, he sends a savior and delivers them (19:20); the LORD makes himself known to Egypt, and Egypt knows the LORD, worships with sacrifice and offering, makes vows and performs them (19:21); they return to him and he listens and heals (19:22). This is covenant vocabulary, the same words Scripture uses for Israel's own salvation, now applied to the empire that once enslaved Israel. There is even an exodus echo in reverse: Egypt oppressed, Egypt crying out, God sending a savior; the roles of Exodus 3:7-10 replayed with Egypt in Israel's part.

"Egypt my people": the highest mission text so far

Then comes the summit. A highway joins Egypt to Assyria, the era's two mortal enemies, "and the Egyptians will worship with the Assyrians" (19:23). And then God does something with his own covenant titles that no Israelite could have predicted: "Blessed be Egypt my people, and Assyria the work of my hands, and Israel my inheritance" (19:25). "My people" is Israel's title from Sinai (Exodus 6:7). "Work of my hands" is Israel's title (Isaiah 60:21). Here they are handed to Egypt and Assyria, while Israel keeps "my inheritance" and becomes "a blessing in the midst of the earth" (19:24), which is Genesis 12:3 coming true: in Abraham's family, all families of the earth blessed. On the course's postmil home lens this is a flagship text: the nations are not merely spared, they are converted, incorporated, and given family names, exactly the trajectory of 2:2-4 and 18:7, and the church has watched it begin: Egypt became one of the great centers of early Christianity. The MacArthur Study Bible notes (ch. 19) place this scene in the future millennial kingdom; amillennial readers like James White read it as the church age in picture form. Whatever your timing, do not miss what the text actually says: Egypt worships, Egypt is healed, Egypt is called "my people." Argue timing later; believe the scope now.

Chapter 20: the acted sermon

Chapter 20 crashes back into datable history: the Ashdod crisis of 713-711 BC, when the Philistine city revolted against Assyria, counting on Egyptian help, and Sargon's commander crushed it. (Verse 1 is the Bible's only mention of Sargon II by name, and for centuries skeptics doubted he existed, until his palace at Khorsabad was dug up in the 1840s with his name on the bricks.) Through those years God had Isaiah perform a sign-act: walking "naked and barefoot," dressed like a prisoner of war, a three-year living billboard with a caption: this is how Assyria will march Egyptian and Cushite captives away, "naked and barefoot, with buttocks uncovered" (20:4). Prophets sometimes preached with their bodies when ears had stopped working (Ezekiel's brick, Jeremiah's yoke, Hosea's marriage). The message lands in 20:6: everyone who leaned on Egypt looks at the chained columns and says, "behold, this is what has happened to those in whom we hoped... and we, how shall we escape?" The nation Judah kept wanting to trust could not even save itself. Hope that is misplaced does not just disappoint; it marches past you in chains.

Holding both halves together

Now put the two Egypt lessons side by side, because the sequence is the theology. The same God, in the same oracle, dismantles Egypt (19:1-15), converts Egypt (19:16-25), and warns Judah not to trust Egypt (ch. 20). No contradiction: Egypt as an object of faith is a broken reed, and Egypt as an object of grace is a future brother. That distinction cuts through the whole Bible: the world is never to be leaned on and always to be evangelized. The remnant principle has quietly gone international: God keeps a people not only out of Jacob, but out of Egypt and Assyria too.

Application

  • About God: he strikes to heal (19:22). His judgments on the nations serve his purpose to be worshiped by them (19:21, 25). No enemy is beyond being renamed "my people."
  • About you: is there a person or group you treat as permanently Egypt, an enemy to outlast rather than a neighbor God may heal? Pray 19:25 over them by name today, and mean it.
  • Do: memorize the three titles of 19:25 in order (Egypt my people, Assyria the work of my hands, Israel my inheritance). Next lesson's recall will ask for them.
