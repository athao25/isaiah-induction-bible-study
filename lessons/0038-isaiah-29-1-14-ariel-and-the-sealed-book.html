Lesson 0038 · Unit 5: Chapters 28-35

Isaiah 29:1-14: Ariel and the Sealed Book

~25 min · Passage: Isaiah 29:1-14 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 29:1-14 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were sent to watch a besieged city called Ariel suddenly rescued, and a vision that has become like a sealed book; keep 29:13 marked. It will follow you into the New Testament.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • A doubled name (29:1): "Ah, Ariel, Ariel, the city where David encamped!" The second woe of the unit opens on David's own city.
  • Feasts on schedule (29:1): "Add year to year; let the feasts run their round." The liturgical calendar keeps turning while the siege gathers.
  • God the besieger (29:2-3): "Yet I will distress Ariel... And I will encamp against you all around, and will besiege you with towers." The commander outside the walls is the LORD.
  • A voice from the dust (29:4): "your voice shall come from the ground like the voice of a ghost." Proud Jerusalem reduced to a whisper.
  • Sudden reversal (29:5): "But the multitude of your foreign foes shall be like small dust... And in an instant, suddenly." The siege breaks in a single verse.
  • A visitation (29:6): "you will be visited by the LORD of hosts with thunder and with earthquake and great noise, with whirlwind and tempest, and the flame of a devouring fire."
  • Enemies like a dream (29:7-8): the nations fighting Ariel become "like a dream, a vision of the night," a hungry man who dreams he eats and wakes with his hunger not quenched.
  • Blindness commanded (29:9): "Astonish yourselves and be astonished; blind yourselves and be blind!" They are told to do it to themselves.
  • Deep sleep poured out (29:10): "the LORD has poured out upon you a spirit of deep sleep, and has closed your eyes (the prophets)."
  • The sealed book (29:11-12): the whole vision handed around; the literate man says "I cannot, for it is sealed," the unlearned says "I cannot read."
  • Lips near, hearts far (29:13): "this people draw near with their mouth and honor me with their lips, while their hearts are far from me, and their fear of me is a commandment taught by men."
  • Wisdom's funeral (29:14): "therefore, behold, I will again do wonderful things with this people... and the wisdom of their wise men shall perish."

Interpretation

Ariel: the altar hearth under siege

The second woe lands where no one expected: not Egypt, not Assyria, but "the city where David encamped" (29:1). Ariel most likely means altar hearth, the top of the altar where the sacrifice burns (Ezekiel 43:15 uses the word that way); Jerusalem is the city with the fire of God at its center, and the name turns grim: the whole city is about to become the hearth, "and she shall be to me like an Ariel" (29:2), burning on her own altar. Watch who commands the siege: "I will encamp against you" (29:3). The same verb as David's encamping in verse 1; the city's founder-God is now its adversary. Unit 3 already taught you there are no exemptions, when Jerusalem stood in the oracle list and took the severest sentence of the section (22:14); here it sharpens: the feasts are running on schedule (29:1) while God builds siegeworks. Religious punctuality is no shield. But then, mid-sentence, the reversal: the horde of foes becomes "small dust... in an instant, suddenly" (29:5), scattered by a visitation of thunder, earthquake, and flame (29:6). Isaiah is previewing a real morning: in 701 BC Assyria's army will ring this city and evaporate overnight, a story this course reaches in chapter 37. And the taunting picture of 29:7-8 will fit it exactly: the nations that fought Ariel wake like a hungry man who only dreamed he ate; Jerusalem in their hands was a dream they never got to keep.

The sealed book and the deep sleep

Verses 9-12 diagnose why the city cannot see any of this coming. Notice the order carefully. First the command, in the plural, addressed to them: "blind yourselves and be blind" (29:9); the stupor begins as their own act, chosen drink by drink and shrug by shrug. Then the sentence: "the LORD has poured out upon you a spirit of deep sleep" (29:10). God's judgment here is to hand people over to the condition they insisted on, confirming the chosen blindness until even the professional seers, "the prophets," have covered eyes. This is the commission of 6:9-10 working itself out: "keep on hearing, but do not understand," the preaching that hardens what it does not heal. And so the vision, all of it, "has become to you like the words of a book that is sealed" (29:11). Hand it to the educated man: "I cannot, for it is sealed." Hand it to the plain man: "I cannot read" (29:12). The book is not actually sealed; Isaiah has been preaching it in the open street for chapters. The problem is not literacy, access, or clarity; it is the heart. Scripture can lie open on a lap and stay shut to a sleeper. That is why every lesson in this course begins with you reading the passage first and praying as you do: the Word opens to the awake.

Lips near, hearts far

Because this people draw near with their mouth and honor me with their lips, while their hearts are far from me, and their fear of me is a commandment taught by men... Isaiah 29:13, ESV

Here is the root under the sealed book, and the verse you were sent to mark. The worship has not stopped; that is the horror of it. The feasts run their round (29:1), the mouths draw near, the lips still honor. Religion can outlive its own sincerity by generations, and the corpse keeps singing. Isaiah names the mechanism: "their fear of me is a commandment taught by men." Reverence learned as technique, passed down as custom, enforced as rule, with no living dread or delight of the Holy One left in it; the seed of every dead tradition ever planted in a living church. Seven centuries later Jesus reaches for exactly this verse when the Pharisees ask why his disciples break the tradition of the elders: "Well did Isaiah prophesy of you hypocrites... This people honors me with their lips, but their heart is far from me" (Matthew 15:7-9; Mark 7:6-7), and he presses Isaiah's diagnosis, "teaching as doctrines the commandments of men." Note what the fix is not: it is not less worship, fewer feasts, emptier liturgy. The distance is not in the form but in the heart's location, "far from me." The fix is a captured heart brought near, which is exactly the wonder God announces next.

Wonder upon wonder

"Therefore, behold, I will again do wonderful things with this people, with wonder upon wonder; and the wisdom of their wise men shall perish" (29:14). When a people manages God instead of fearing him, files him under custom and committee, God's answer is to act in a way their categories cannot process. The wise men of Jerusalem, the strategists steering toward an Egypt alliance behind closed doors (next lesson meets them), will watch their statecraft evaporate and an angel do in one night what no policy could. And the pattern scales. Paul reaches for this very verse to explain the cross: "I will destroy the wisdom of the wise" (1 Corinthians 1:19, quoting 29:14); the crucified Messiah, "folly to those who are perishing... the power of God" to the saved, is the wonder upon wonder that buried the wisdom of the wise for good. The MacArthur Study Bible notes (your copy) trace both the Assyrian deliverance and the messianic horizon here; on any lens, the through-line holds: God will not be managed, and his rescue arrives in forms that make experts start over like children. That is bad news for the self-assured and very good news for the ones whispering from the dust (29:4).

Memory verse review
No new verse today; verse 8 arrives at chapter 30. Instead recite two from memory, references included: Isaiah 26:3 and Isaiah 7:9b. Say them back to back and notice they are the same lesson from two directions: where trust rests, the mind stands or falls. Schedule and method: memory verses.

Application

  • About God: near lips do not fool him; he reads hearts at any distance (29:13), and he can still scatter a besieging army "in an instant, suddenly" (29:5). Neither your performance nor your predicament is what it appears.
  • About you: find the place your worship has gone procedural: the same words in the same slot with your heart elsewhere. Then name one "commandment taught by men" you have quietly promoted to Scripture, a rule or custom you hold with biblical force that the Bible never gave you.
  • Do: before Sunday, take one prayer you pray on autopilot (before meals, at bedtime, in the service) and rewrite it in your own fresh words; pray the new one all week. Bring 29:13 to your church conversation and ask each other honestly where your congregation's reverence runs on inheritance instead of encounter.
