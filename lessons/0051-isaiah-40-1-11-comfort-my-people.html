Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • The doubled opening (40:1): "Comfort, comfort my people, says your God." The first word of the book's second half is repeated, and it is not a threat.
  • Speak to her heart (40:2): "speak tenderly to Jerusalem" is literally "speak to her heart"; the message has three parts: warfare ended, iniquity pardoned, "received from the LORD's hand double for all her sins."
  • A voice in the wilderness (40:3): "A voice cries: 'In the wilderness prepare the way of the LORD; make straight in the desert a highway for our God.'"
  • Earthmoving orders (40:4): "Every valley shall be lifted up, and every mountain and hill be made low; the uneven ground shall become level." The terrain itself is rearranged.
  • Glory for all flesh (40:5): "the glory of the LORD shall be revealed, and all flesh shall see it together, for the mouth of the LORD has spoken."
  • A second voice (40:6): a voice says "Cry!" and the answer comes back, "What shall I cry? All flesh is grass."
  • The breath that withers (40:7): "The grass withers, the flower fades when the breath of the LORD blows on it; surely the people are grass."
  • The word that stands (40:8): "The grass withers, the flower fades, but the word of our God will stand forever."
  • Zion the herald (40:9): "Go on up to a high mountain, O Zion, herald of good news... lift up your voice with strength... say to the cities of Judah, 'Behold your God!'" The comforted one is handed the megaphone.
  • The ruling arm (40:10): "the Lord GOD comes with might, and his arm rules for him; behold, his reward is with him."
  • The carrying arm (40:11): "He will tend his flock like a shepherd; he will gather the lambs in his arms; he will carry them in his bosom, and gently lead those that are with young."

Interpretation

The hinge crossed: comfort for people not yet born

Chapter 39 ended with the worst sentence in the book so far: everything to Babylon, sons taken. Chapter 40 opens God's answer, and notice who is being answered. The audience quietly shifts: Isaiah is now speaking past his own generation to exiles a century and a half downstream, people for whom every judgment sermon of chapters 1-39 has already come true, who sit in Babylon wondering if the covenant died with the city. That predictive stance is not a problem to explain away; it is the book's own claim, and Isaiah is about to hammer it for eight chapters as the very proof that the LORD is God and the idols are not: "declaring the end from the beginning" (46:10). The New Testament reads it the same way, quoting both halves of the book as the words of the one prophet Isaiah (John 12:38-41 cites Isaiah 53 and Isaiah 6 together). And what does this God say to the people his own sentence crushed? "Comfort, comfort." The language doubles a word to intensify it; you have met shalom shalom (26:3) and Ariel, Ariel (29:1). Comfort squared. Better still, hear the pronouns: "MY people... YOUR God" (40:1). The exile the covenant decreed did not dissolve the covenant. And the tender word to Jerusalem's heart declares the sentence fully served: "she has received from the LORD's hand double for all her sins" (40:2), sufficiency language, ample and complete, nothing outstanding on the account. Comfort here is not a mood; it is a legal announcement: warfare ended, iniquity pardoned, sentence served.

The voice and the highway

A voice cries: "In the wilderness prepare the way of the LORD; make straight in the desert a highway for our God." Isaiah 40:3, ESV

You have watched the highway theme build across the book: a highway from Assyria for the remnant (11:16), the Way of Holiness through the blooming desert (35:8). Now it becomes a construction order. Valleys up, mountains down, rough places plain (40:4): the terrain itself is regraded, because the King is coming and nothing may slow his arrival. Then mark what every Gospel writer marks: all four Gospels put this verse on John the Baptist (Matthew 3:3, Mark 1:3, Luke 3:4-6, John 1:23). John is the voice; the wilderness is Judea's; and the LORD whose way he prepared arrived as Jesus of Nazareth. This is one of Scripture's plainest identifications of Jesus with the God of Isaiah: the highway built for Yahweh got walked by Christ. And verse 5 sets the blast radius: "the glory of the LORD shall be revealed, and all flesh shall see it together." Comfort spoken to one ruined city ends up seen by everyone; the scope you watched at the feast (25:6) and the filled world (27:6) holds here too.

Grass and the word that stands

The grass withers, the flower fades, but the word of our God will stand forever. Isaiah 40:8, ESV

Your new memory verse, and it begins with brutal honesty. The second voice commands "Cry!", and the crier answers with a question and a verdict: "What shall I cry? All flesh is grass" (40:6). Not wicked flesh only; all of it. Beauty like wildflowers, gone at the first hot wind, "when the breath of the LORD blows on it" (40:7). Empires included: you just watched the proof, for Assyria's army was mown down in a single night (37:36), and Babylon's turn is already on the calendar (39:6-7; 46-47 will preach its funeral in advance). If your comfort rests on anything made of flesh, on any leader, economy, institution, or on your own strength, it rests on grass. Against every fading thing the verse sets one permanence: "the word of our God will stand forever." The promise outlives the promisers and the threateners alike. Peter quotes 40:6-8 whole and then adds the punchline: "And this word is the good news that was preached to you" (1 Peter 1:24-25). The everlasting word is not an abstraction; it is the gospel in your hands. This is why comfort could be commanded in verse 1 at all: it rests on the one thing in the universe that cannot wither.

Behold your God: the arm that rules and carries

Verse 9 hands the comforted one the megaphone: "Go on up to a high mountain, O Zion, herald of good news... say to the cities of Judah, 'Behold your God!'" Good news is for announcing; this "herald of good news" vocabulary is the very lane where the word "gospel" lives, and Isaiah will drive down it again at 52:7 and 61:1. Comfort received becomes comfort proclaimed, or it curdles. Then the double portrait, and read the two verses as one sentence. Verse 10: "his arm rules for him," the mighty arm, reward in hand, the strength that flattened Assyria. Verse 11: the same God "will tend his flock like a shepherd; he will gather the lambs in his arms; he will carry them in his bosom, and gently lead those that are with young." The arm that rules is the arm under the lamb. Power and tenderness are not in tension in this God; each makes the other good news, for a tender God without might could pity you but not save you, and a mighty God without tenderness could save you but might not want to. Jesus stands squarely on this verse: "I am the good shepherd" (John 10:11), the one who lays the found sheep on his shoulders rejoicing (Luke 15:5). The MacArthur Study Bible notes (your copy) are especially strong on this chapter's comfort structure; read them with the Gospel texts open.

Application

  • About God: he commands comfort because the sentence is served (40:2), builds highways through wildernesses no road should cross (40:3-4), and carries lambs with the same arm that rules the nations (40:10-11). Nothing about your rescue is halfhearted.
  • About you: audit which voice you have been listening to lately: the withering-grass news cycle, or the word that stands forever. And ask whether you have ever moved from comforted to herald (40:9); comfort that never gets announced was never fully believed.
  • Do: start each day this week by reading 40:1-11 aloud before you open any feed or inbox; let the standing word get the first word. Then deliver one "behold your God" sentence of actual good news to one discouraged person this week, plainly, out loud.
