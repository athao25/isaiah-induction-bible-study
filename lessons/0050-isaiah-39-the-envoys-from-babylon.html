Lesson 0050 · Unit 6: Chapters 36-39

Isaiah 39: The Envoys from Babylon

~25 min · Passage: Isaiah 39:1-8 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 39 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were sent to watch who visits, what Hezekiah shows them, Isaiah's sentence word by word, and Hezekiah's final reply. Unit 6 closes today.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • A Babylonian get-well card (39:1): "Merodach-baladan the son of Baladan, king of Babylon, sent envoys with letters and a present to Hezekiah, for he heard that he had been sick and had recovered."
  • Gladly (39:2): "And Hezekiah welcomed them gladly." One adverb carries the whole chapter.
  • The full inventory (39:2): "the silver, the gold, the spices, the precious oil, his whole armory, all that was found in his storehouses."
  • The narrator's underline (39:2): "There was nothing in his house or in all his realm that Hezekiah did not show them." The text says it twice so you cannot miss it.
  • The prophet arrives asking (39:3): "What did these men say? And from where did they come to you?" Isaiah comes to the king with questions, not greetings.
  • A half-answer (39:3): "They have come to me from a far country, from Babylon." Hezekiah answers where they came from, not what they said.
  • The second question (39:4): "What have they seen in your house?"
  • The full confession (39:4): "They have seen all that is in my house. There is nothing in my storehouses that I did not show them."
  • The word of the LORD of hosts (39:5): "Hear the word of the LORD of hosts." The formal formula: what follows is sentence, not suggestion.
  • Everything carried off (39:6): "all that is in your house... shall be carried to Babylon. Nothing shall be left, says the LORD."
  • Sons taken (39:7): "And some of your own sons... shall be taken away, and they shall be eunuchs in the palace of the king of Babylon."
  • The last words of the story (39:8): "The word of the LORD that you have spoken is good." For he thought, "There will be peace and security in my days."

Interpretation

A get-well card with a treaty inside

Start with the sender. Merodach-baladan was no idle well-wisher; he was Assyria's most persistent Babylonian rebel, a chieftain who seized Babylon's throne twice and spent his career recruiting partners for revolt. His envoys carry letters and a present because Hezekiah has just become interesting: a king who survived his illness, and whose God moves shadows on dials. The get-well card has a treaty inside; this visit is coalition-shopping dressed as courtesy. And Hezekiah, fresh from two miracles, fifteen added years and a shadow walked backward, "welcomed them gladly" and showed everything: silver, gold, spices, oil, armory, storehouses. Scripture supplies its own commentary on the scene:

And so in the matter of the envoys of the princes of Babylon, who had been sent to him to inquire about the sign that had been done in the land, God left him to himself, in order to test him and to know all that was in his heart. 2 Chronicles 32:31, ESV

Chronicles adds that after his healing "his heart was proud" (2 Chronicles 32:25). Here is the sobering pattern: the man who survived the Rabshakeh's threats is undone by Babylon's flattery. Threats drove him to the temple; compliments drove him to the vault. The siege could not break his trust, but a delegation of admirers broke it in an afternoon. Prosperity is a test too, and often the harder one, because nobody spreads a compliment before the LORD.

The prophet's questions

Isaiah walks in interrogating like God in Eden: "Where are you?... Who told you?" (Genesis 3:9-11). The prophet's questions, "What did these men say? And from where did they come to you?... What have they seen in your house?", are not requests for information; God's man already knows the answers, and the questions exist to make the king say them out loud. Notice Hezekiah's first reply is a half-answer: he names where the envoys came from and skips what they said, the diplomatic content he would rather not repeat to a prophet who has spent thirty chapters condemning foreign alliances. The second question corners him, and to his credit he confesses in full: "There is nothing... that I did not show them." Now weigh what he showed against what he did not. The storehouses, not the temple. The treasure, not the testimony. The armory, not the altar. Nobody took the envoys to see the sundial where God moved the shadow, or told them about the angel and the 185,000. Babylon came asking about a sign (2 Chronicles 32:31) and got a tour of assets instead. It is a question worth sitting with: when impressive people visit your life, what do you give the tour of?

The sentence, and the hinge of the book

Behold, the days are coming, when all that is in your house, and that which your fathers have stored up till this day, shall be carried to Babylon. Nothing shall be left, says the LORD. And some of your own sons, who will come from you, whom you will father, shall be taken away, and they shall be eunuchs in the palace of the king of Babylon. Isaiah 39:6-7, ESV

Measure the reach of this word. Babylon in Hezekiah's day was a subject state, a rebel province of Assyria; yet Isaiah names it as the empire of exile more than a century before Jerusalem falls to it in 586 BC. The fulfillment is itemized in 2 Kings 24:13 and 25:13-17, where the treasuries and the temple vessels go to Babylon exactly as spoken, and "some of your own sons... eunuchs in the palace" lands on Daniel and his royal-blooded friends, carried off to serve in the palace of the king of Babylon (Daniel 1:3-7). This is the book's load-bearing hinge. Chapters 1-39 close with the word Babylon hanging in the air over everything Hezekiah owns; chapter 40 will open with God answering it: "Comfort, comfort my people." Then weigh 39:8 fairly, because it cuts two ways. "The word of the LORD that you have spoken is good" is real submission; Eli said the like (1 Samuel 3:18). But the narrator lets us hear the king's inner arithmetic: "There will be peace and security in my days." Submission shades into relieved self-interest. The intercessor of chapter 37, who spread a letter before the LORD for the city's sake, has stopped praying for the generations after him. Set his last recorded thought beside his own psalm from last lesson: "the father makes known to the children your faithfulness" (38:19). The healed king sang it; the flattered king forgot it.

Closing Unit 6: history at the hinge

Step back; the four history chapters ran two tests on one king, and the tests rhyme. Test one: an Assyrian letter full of threats. Hezekiah took it to the temple and spread it before the LORD (37:14), and God saved the city by an angel in a night. Test two: Babylonian letters full of flattery. Hezekiah spread his treasures before the envoys, and God decreed the exile. Same verb-shape, opposite direction: what you spread before the LORD he defends; what you spread before Babylon, Babylon eventually collects. Three threads to carry out of the unit. First, the trust question of Unit 5 got its historical answer: "in returning and rest you shall be saved" (30:15) was not theory; chapters 36-37 showed God really saving those who rest on him, against the largest army on earth. Second, even the best king fails the exit interview: Hezekiah prayed like David and sang like David, and still ended proud, short-sighted, and content with "peace in my days." If the finest king since David cannot carry the promise, the book must look past him, to the King who reigns in righteousness (32:1) and the Servant about to step forward. Third, the enemy changes names: Assyria the rod is broken and gone; Babylon the exile-house steps out of the wings. That is why the Book of Comfort (chapters 40-66) speaks over the horizon to captives not yet born. The MacArthur Study Bible notes (your copy) mark this same turn: judgment has said its piece, and comfort takes the microphone. Next lesson opens the second half of the book: "Comfort, comfort my people, says your God."

Application

  • About God: he tests hearts with prosperity as surely as with siege (2 Chronicles 32:31), and he answers pride's exile with a word of comfort written a century early. Even his severest sentence arrives with chapter 40 already drafted.
  • About you: when impressive people visit your life, what do you show first: storehouse or sundial, resume or testimony? And where has "peace in my days" quietly ended your praying: which person or generation after you have you stopped carrying to God?
  • Do: run one deliberate inversion this week. In one conversation where you would normally display an asset, a win, a purchase, a credential, tell the story of a rescue instead. And pray by name, today and each day this week, for someone two generations younger than you.
