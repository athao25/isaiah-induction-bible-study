Lesson 0036 · Unit 4: Chapters 24-27

Isaiah 27: The Vineyard Sings Again

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 27:1-13 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 27 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were sent to find a vineyard song that answers chapter 5 and a serpent named Leviathan; keep both marked. Unit 4 closes today.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • "In that day" three times (27:1, 2, 12): the chapter is stitched to the whole unit's day of the LORD with the section's signature phrase.
  • Leviathan slain (27:1): "the LORD with his hard and great and strong sword will punish Leviathan the fleeing serpent, Leviathan the twisting serpent, and he will slay the dragon that is in the sea."
  • A vineyard again (27:2-3): "A pleasant vineyard, sing of it! I, the LORD, am its keeper; every moment I water it." You have heard a vineyard song before, in chapter 5.
  • No wrath (27:4): "I have no wrath. Would that I had thorns and briers to battle!" Compare that to what the vineyard got in 5:6.
  • Peace offered twice (27:5): "let them lay hold of my protection, let them make peace with me, let them make peace with me." The offer repeats itself.
  • The world filled (27:6): "In days to come Jacob shall take root, Israel shall blossom and put forth shoots and fill the whole world with fruit."
  • Measured striking (27:7-8): "Has he struck them as he struck those who struck them?... Measure by measure, by exile you contended with them." God disciplines his people differently than he destroys his enemies.
  • Guilt atoned, idols crushed (27:9): "by this the guilt of Jacob will be atoned for... when he makes all the stones of the altars like chalkstones crushed to pieces, no Asherim or incense altars will remain."
  • A city left solitary (27:10-11): "the fortified city is solitary... there the calf grazes." And a hard line: "this is a people without discernment; therefore he who made them will not have compassion on them."
  • Gleaned one by one (27:12): "you will be gleaned one by one, O people of Israel." Not scooped up in bulk; gathered individually.
  • A great trumpet (27:13): "those who were lost in the land of Assyria and those who were driven out to the land of Egypt will come and worship the LORD on the holy mountain at Jerusalem."

Interpretation

Leviathan: the serpent finally slain

In that day the LORD with his hard and great and strong sword will punish Leviathan the fleeing serpent, Leviathan the twisting serpent, and he will slay the dragon that is in the sea. Isaiah 27:1, ESV

Who is Leviathan? Isaiah's first hearers knew the name well. In the sea myths of Israel's neighbors, a chaos serpent of the deep (the Canaanite texts call him Lotan, "the fleeing serpent, the twisting serpent," nearly word for word what Isaiah writes) fights the storm god for control of the world. Isaiah picks up the pagan poem and files it under "things the LORD will actually do": the sea monster the nations feared is just another creature under his sword. Scripture uses the figure for the chaos powers behind history, sometimes wearing a national face (Egypt is "the dragon" in 30:7 and 51:9), and behind every face, the oldest serpent of all. Genesis promised the serpent's head would be crushed (Genesis 3:15); Revelation names "the dragon, that ancient serpent, who is the devil" and shows him thrown down and finally destroyed (Revelation 12:9, 20:2, 20:10). The MacArthur Study Bible notes (your copy) take 27:1 as Satan's destruction at the end of the future tribulation; on any lens, the New Testament adds that the decisive blow has already landed at the cross, where Christ "disarmed the rulers and authorities" (Colossians 2:15) and broke "the one who has the power of death, that is, the devil" (Hebrews 2:14). The day of the LORD does not merely rearrange nations; it kills the snake.

The vineyard sings again

Now the song you were sent to find. In chapter 5 Isaiah sang of a vineyard that got every advantage and yielded wild grapes, so the owner tore out its hedge, forbade the rain, and let briers and thorns take it (5:1-7). Chapter 27 sings the same vineyard from the far side of judgment, and every line answers a line from the old song. Then: "I will remove its hedge" (5:5); now: "I, the LORD, am its keeper; every moment I water it... I keep it night and day" (27:3). Then: "briers and thorns shall grow up" as the sentence (5:6); now briers and thorns are God's enemies, and he marches against them (27:4). Then: wrath; now, astonishingly: "I have no wrath" (27:4). The judgment fell, the fury of 26:20 passed, and what is left is a keeper who cannot stop tending his vines. And the offer inside the song repeats itself like a father calling twice: "let them make peace with me, let them make peace with me" (27:5). Even the thorns can switch sides. Then verse 6 lifts the camera: "Jacob shall take root, Israel shall blossom and put forth shoots and fill the whole world with fruit." The vineyard that once produced bloodshed instead of justice (5:7) becomes the world's food supply. On our postmil home lens this is a flagship text: the story arcs not toward a rescued remnant huddled small but toward a planted people whose fruit fills the earth, the same trajectory as "Egypt my people" (19:24-25) and the feast for all peoples (25:6). Jesus stands in this vineyard when he says "I am the true vine, and my Father is the vinedresser" (John 15:1); fruit that fills the world is what the keeper has always been after.

Measure by measure

Verses 7-11 slow down and ask a careful question: "Has he struck them as he struck those who struck them?" (27:7). No. When God judged Babylon, Babylon ended; when God judged Israel, the blow was dosed: "measure by measure, by exile you contended with them" (27:8). Discipline is not destruction. And it has a stated goal: "by this the guilt of Jacob will be atoned for" (27:9), and the proof of the medicine working is smashed idolatry, altar stones ground to chalk, no Asherim left standing. Be careful with the word "atoned" here: the exile did not pay for sin (only a sacrifice does that, and Isaiah will unveil the true one in chapter 53); it was the furnace in which God burned idolatry out of his people, and historically it worked, for Israel returned from Babylon permanently cured of carved gods. The solitary city of 27:10-11, deserted, calves grazing in its ruins, is the other outcome: the world-city of this unit (24:10, 25:2, 26:5), left to the fate the vineyard was spared. And verse 11's hard sentence, "a people without discernment... he who made them will show them no favor," is there so you never treat the vineyard's mercy as automatic. Same fire; the difference is whether it refines you or ends you.

Gleaned one by one, gathered by trumpet

And in that day a great trumpet will be blown, and those who were lost in the land of Assyria and those who were driven out to the land of Egypt will come and worship the LORD on the holy mountain at Jerusalem. Isaiah 27:13, ESV

The unit ends with harvest. "You will be gleaned one by one" (27:12): threshing normally moves in bulk, but this harvester goes through the field by hand, from the Euphrates to the Brook of Egypt, and no single grain is left behind or gathered by accident. Salvation is corporate in scope and personal in method; the feast of 25:6 seats a crowd, but every chair has a name. Then the great trumpet (27:13), the sound that in Israel announced Jubilee liberty (Leviticus 25:9), summons the lost from Assyria and the driven-out from Egypt, the two superpowers that swallowed God's people, and brings them to worship "on the holy mountain at Jerusalem," the very mountain where the feast was spread and death was swallowed (25:6-8). The New Testament picks up the horn: the Son of Man sends his angels "with a loud trumpet call" to gather the elect (Matthew 24:31), and the dead in Christ rise "at the last trumpet" (1 Corinthians 15:52; 1 Thessalonians 4:16). However the lenses time it, the shape holds: the same God who slays the dragon and keeps the vineyard also walks the rows at the end, picking up his own one by one.

Closing Unit 4: Isaiah's apocalypse in four movements

Step back; you have walked the whole unit, chapters 24-27, sometimes called Isaiah's apocalypse. The movements: chapter 24 laid the earth waste and broke the city of chaos, because the everlasting covenant was broken (24:5); chapter 25 answered with a feast on the mountain and the promise that death itself would be swallowed forever (25:6-8); chapter 26 sang the two cities, taught a kept mind perfect peace (26:3), and promised bodies out of dust (26:19); chapter 27 slays the serpent, re-sings the vineyard with no wrath left, and gleans the world one by one to the mountain. Four threads to hold. First, judgment goes cosmic: the oracles of Unit 3 named nations; here the whole earth stands in the dock. Second, every judgment scene breaks into song: 24:14-16, 25:1, 26:1, 27:2; in this unit nobody sings until judgment has done its work, and then nobody can stop. Third, the last enemy is death, and it loses: swallowed (25:8), out-sung by resurrection (26:19), its serpent slain (27:1). Fourth, the end is a gathering: all peoples at one table, one by one to one mountain. The eschatology fork you opened at chapter 24 (lesson 33) stays honestly open: MacArthur's premil reading places the unit at the future tribulation and millennium; White's amil reading hears final judgment and the eternal state; the postmil home lens reads history bending through real judgments toward the feast, the full vineyard, and the filled earth. Where all three lock arms: the serpent dies, death dies, and the table gets set. Next, Isaiah comes back down from the cosmic summit to street level: six woes for leaders trusting the wrong helpers, chapters 28-33, and inside them a cornerstone.

Application

  • About God: he slays the chaos serpent (27:1), waters his vineyard every moment and keeps it night and day (27:3), and gleans his people one by one (27:12). Cosmic enough to kill dragons, attentive enough to know your row.
  • About you: the offer of 27:5 is doubled for a reason: "let them make peace with me." Name the one place you are still playing the brier, resisting the keeper of the vineyard, and take the offer there this week.
  • Do: pray 27:2-6 over your own household as the keeper's song. Then practice the one-by-one gleaning: name one person "lost in Assyria," far off and swallowed by something bigger than them, and commit to pray them toward the mountain daily through Unit 5.
