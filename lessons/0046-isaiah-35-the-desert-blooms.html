Lesson 0046 · Unit 5: Chapters 28-35

Isaiah 35: The Desert Blooms

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 35:1-10 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 35 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were sent to watch blind eyes, deaf ears, and lame legs, and to mark the named highway of 35:8 and who may walk it. Keep those marked. Unit 5 closes today.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • The desert rejoices (35:1): "The wilderness and the dry land shall be glad; the desert shall rejoice and blossom like the crocus." Sand is singing.
  • Blossoming doubled (35:2): "it shall blossom abundantly and rejoice with joy and singing." The language piles up joy on joy.
  • Borrowed glory (35:2): "The glory of Lebanon shall be given to it, the majesty of Carmel and Sharon." The exact regions that withered in 33:9 are restored, and more: "They shall see the glory of the LORD."
  • Weak hands, feeble knees (35:3): "Strengthen the weak hands, and make firm the feeble knees." A command to somebody; notice it is plural work.
  • A word for the anxious (35:4): "Say to those who have an anxious heart, 'Be strong; fear not! Behold, your God will come with vengeance... He will come and save you.'"
  • Four healings (35:5-6): eyes of the blind opened, ears of the deaf unstopped, the lame man leaping like a deer, the mute tongue singing for joy.
  • Water in the wilderness (35:6): "waters break forth in the wilderness, and streams in the desert."
  • Jackal haunts converted (35:7): "the burning sand shall become a pool... in the haunt of jackals... the grass shall become reeds and rushes." Chapter 34 gave Edom to the jackals (34:13); here their haunt becomes a wetland.
  • A named highway (35:8): "a highway shall be there... it shall be called the Way of Holiness; the unclean shall not pass over it."
  • Safe for fools (35:8): "it shall belong to those who walk on the way; even if they are fools, they shall not go astray."
  • No lions (35:9): "No lion shall be there, nor shall any ravenous beast come up on it... but the redeemed shall walk there."
  • The ransomed come home (35:10): "the ransomed of the LORD shall return and come to Zion with singing; everlasting joy shall be upon their heads... and sorrow and sighing shall flee away."

Interpretation

The other desert

Read chapters 34 and 35 as one diptych, two panels hinged together. In chapter 34 a populated land ran backward into chaos: streams to pitch, soil to sulfur, palaces to thorns, the measuring lines of "confusion and emptiness" stretched over it (34:11), and the jackals moved in (34:13). In chapter 35 the film runs the other way: a desert floods into bloom, and the reversals are point for point. The haunt of jackals becomes grass, reeds, and rushes (35:7). Lebanon, Carmel, and Sharon, the three glories that withered and shook off their leaves under the destroyer in 33:9, get their majesty handed back (35:2). Same God, two deserts, opposite verdicts, and the difference is not geography or climate; it is grace. One land is de-created because it set itself against Zion; another is re-created because God is coming through it to save. Isaiah has been drawing this line since the two cities of chapters 24-27: there is no neutral ground, only land that ends in jackals and land that ends in crocuses.

Say to the anxious heart

Strengthen the weak hands, and make firm the feeble knees. Say to those who have an anxious heart, "Be strong; fear not! Behold, your God will come with vengeance, with the recompense of God. He will come and save you." Isaiah 35:3-4, ESV

Notice who these commands are addressed to: not the weak-handed themselves but the people around them. Strengthening hands, firming knees, and speaking to anxious hearts is community work; somebody's courage is assigned to you. The letter to the Hebrews quotes verse 3 exactly this way, as a word to weary Christians mid-marathon: "lift your drooping hands and strengthen your weak knees" (Hebrews 12:12-13). And listen to the reason fear is allowed to leave: "your God will come with vengeance... He will come and save you." That sentence welds chapters 34 and 35 into one event. The vengeance that flattened Edom and the salvation that makes deserts sing are not two comings but one arrival seen from two sides; the same fire that ends the destroyer thaws the desert. You cannot have a Savior who never judges, because rescuing the oppressed and stopping the oppressor are a single act.

The Messiah's ID card

Then the eyes of the blind shall be opened, and the ears of the deaf unstopped; then shall the lame man leap like a deer, and the tongue of the mute sing for joy. Isaiah 35:5-6, ESV

Hold this list in your hand and open Matthew 11:2-6. John the Baptist, in prison and doubting, sends to ask Jesus: "Are you the one who is to come?" Jesus does not answer with a yes. He answers with a performance and a quotation: "the blind receive their sight and the lame walk... the deaf hear" (Matthew 11:5; Luke 7:18-23 adds that he healed many "in that hour" before replying). Jesus treats Isaiah 35 as his own job description; the checklist for the day when "your God will come... and save you" is the checklist he is visibly ticking. Which means his healing miracles were never stunts or mere proofs of power; each one was a preview of Isaiah 35, a square foot of desert blooming early. And it means the blooming age is not only future: it broke in when the Messiah came. The MacArthur Study Bible notes (your copy) place the chapter's fulfillment in the future millennium; the course's postmil lens, with Matthew 11:5 in hand, sees the bloom begun at the first coming and consummated at the return. Both lenses agree on the substance: the desert really blooms, and the ransomed really come home singing.

The Way of Holiness

Through the blooming desert runs a road, and it has a name: "the Way of Holiness" (35:8). You have met this highway before; it is one of Isaiah's theme roads, the remnant's highway out of Assyria (11:16), the highway between Egypt and Assyria on which enemies travel to worship together (19:23), and in five chapters a voice will cry "prepare the way of the LORD; make straight in the desert a highway" (40:3). Now look at its two guardrails, held together. On one side: "the unclean shall not pass over it" (35:8); holiness is not optional equipment for this road. On the other: "even if they are fools, they shall not go astray" (35:8), and "no lion shall be there" (35:9), the predator imagery of the unit retired. So the holiness of this way is not gate-keeping snobbery that admits only the competent; it is protection. The road itself is walled so well, and grace guides so firmly, that even a fool cannot manage to get lost on it. Who walks it? "The redeemed... the ransomed of the LORD" (35:9-10), people defined not by their navigation skills but by the price paid for them. And the destination is singing: "everlasting joy shall be upon their heads... and sorrow and sighing shall flee away" (35:10). Isaiah will repeat that verse word for word at 51:11; some promises deserve saying twice.

Closing Unit 5: six woes and the true refuge

Step back; you have walked the whole unit, chapters 28-35. The arc, woe by woe: the drunk crowns of Ephraim and the scoffers' covenant with death, answered by a tested cornerstone in Zion (28); Ariel besieged, her seers sealed shut (29:1-14); the potter overruled by his clay, then deaf ears opened and eyes unsealed (29:15-24); the embassy south to Egypt, answered by the offer Judah refused, "in returning and rest you shall be saved" (30:1-17), and then the God who waits to be gracious (30:18-33); horses and chariots against the LORD who hovers over Zion like a lioness and like birds (31); the King who reigns in righteousness and the Spirit poured from on high (32); the destroyer destroyed and the King seen in his beauty (33); then the two deserts, one de-created (34), one blooming (35). Four threads to hold. First, every false refuge in the unit is a fast, loud alternative to a slow, quiet trust: horses are quicker than waiting, treaties louder than prayer. Second, the unit's spine is your memory verse, 30:15, and its answer is 32:15-17: the quietness and trust that the command demanded, the poured-out Spirit finally grows. Third, judgment and salvation keep arriving as one event, from the cornerstone that saves believers and breaks scoffers to the vengeance-and-rescue of 35:4. Fourth, the exam question of the entire unit, "in whom do you now trust?", is about to be asked out loud, at a wall, by an Assyrian officer, because next unit the poetry becomes history and the theology gets a live test.

Application

  • About God: his vengeance and his saving are one coming (35:4); the fire that stops the destroyer is the warmth that blooms the desert. And his holiness builds a road fools cannot fall off (35:8); grace does the navigating.
  • About you: verses 3-4 assign you somebody. Whose weak hands and feeble knees are within your reach this week, and what would "Be strong; fear not" sound like in your voice, said out loud to their face, not posted or texted?
  • Do: deliver 35:3-4 in person to one anxious believer this week; say the verses and name why fear can leave. Then reread Matthew 11:2-6 and write down what evidence of the blooming age you have personally seen: healings, conversions, deserts of any kind turned to reeds and rushes.
