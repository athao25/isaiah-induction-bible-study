Lesson 0032 · Unit 3: Chapters 13-23

Isaiah 23: The Oracle Against Tyre

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 23:1-18 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 23 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. You were asked to watch what happens to Tyre's profits in the last verse; hold onto that, it is the strangest ending of any oracle in the book.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • Ships wailing (23:1, 14): the oracle opens and closes with the same cry, "Wail, O ships of Tarshish," a frame around the whole chapter. The long-haul merchant fleet learns in port that home harbor is gone.
  • An economy described (23:2-3): Sidon's merchants, "the grain of Shihor... the harvest of the Nile," Tyre as "the merchant of the nations." This oracle is about trade the way Babylon's was about empire.
  • Merchant princes (23:8): "whose merchants were princes, whose traders were the honored of the earth." Commerce had crowned its own royalty.
  • The purpose named (23:9): "The LORD of hosts has purposed it, to defile the pompous pride of all glory, to dishonor all the honored of the earth."
  • No rest anywhere (23:12): flee to Cyprus, "even there you will have no rest." Money can buy passage but not peace.
  • Seventy years (23:15): "Tyre will be forgotten for seventy years, like the days of one king." Another dated, checkable clock, like Moab's three years (16:14) and Kedar's one (21:16).
  • The prostitute's song (23:15-16): forgotten Tyre is pictured as an aging prostitute walking the city with a harp, singing to be remembered.
  • Back to business (23:17): after seventy years Tyre "will return to her wages and will prostitute herself with all the kingdoms of the world." Trade resumes, unconverted.
  • The shock ending (23:18): "Her merchandise and her wages will be holy to the LORD... her merchandise will supply abundant food and fine clothing for those who dwell before the LORD." The profits end up consecrated.

Interpretation

The last oracle: money on trial

Tyre closes the oracle collection deliberately. Babylon opened it (ch. 13-14) as the pride of political empire; Tyre ends it as the pride of commerce. Tyre was an island fortress off the Phoenician coast whose real weapons were fleets and ledgers: her merchants were princes (23:8), her colonies (like Tarshish, probably in Spain) ringed the Mediterranean. Isaiah has no quarrel with ships or trade as such; the charge is in verse 9, and it is the thesis of the whole eleven-chapter section: "to defile the pompous pride of all glory, to dishonor all the honored of the earth." Compare 2:12-17, where the day of the LORD came against "all the ships of Tarshish" among the lofty things. Wherever glory sets up as self-made, whether it sails or reigns, the LORD of hosts has purposed to bring it down.

Seventy years, and a strange restoration

Her merchandise and her wages will be holy to the LORD. It will not be stored or hoarded, but her merchandise will supply abundant food and fine clothing for those who dwell before the LORD. Isaiah 23:18, ESV

The oracle ends twice, and the two endings must be read together. First: after seventy years, "like the days of one king" (a full royal lifespan, 23:15), Tyre returns to her trade, and Isaiah calls that trade prostitution (23:17): commerce without conscience, intimacy with every kingdom for a fee. History obliged; Tyre was humbled in the Assyrian and Babylonian periods and returned to prosperity. But then verse 18: the wages of that very trade "will be holy to the LORD," not hoarded but feeding and clothing "those who dwell before the LORD." The unclean earnings end up on the altar. This is the same motion you saw with Egypt and Assyria (19:24-25): judgment is not God's last word about the nations, and it is not his last word about their economies either. Isaiah will finish the thought in 60:5-11, where "the wealth of the nations" and "the ships of Tarshish first" stream into Zion. On our postmil home lens this is a load-bearing text: the gospel does not abolish trade, it converts it; markets, fleets, and wages are redeemable, and history is headed toward their consecration. The MacArthur Study Bible notes (ch. 23) place verse 18's fulfillment in the future millennium; notice that Tyrians served Zion's rebuilding already in Ezra 3:7, and weigh whether the consecration is only future or already underway wherever converted wealth serves God's house.

Closing Unit 3: the arc of the oracles

You have now walked the whole collection, chapters 13-23. Look back at the map: Babylon (13-14), Assyria and Philistia (14), Moab (15-16), Damascus and Ephraim (17), Cush (18), Egypt (19-20), Babylon again by night-watch (21), Dumah and Arabia (21), Jerusalem herself (22), and Tyre (23). Four threads ran through everything. First, pride humbled: every oracle is 2:11-17 applied to a new address, and 23:9 says so outright. Second, no exemptions: the list includes God's own city (22), which got the severest sentence in the section (22:14). Third, dated, checkable words: three years for Moab, one for Kedar, seventy for Tyre; this prophet signs his work. Fourth, and least expected, mercy leaking through judgment: a throne in David's tent inside the Moab oracle (16:5), "Egypt my people" inside Egypt's (19:25), consecrated wages inside Tyre's (23:18), and shelter for the outcast throughout. The nations are judged, and the nations are wanted. The remnant logic of chapter 10 turns out to apply to the whole world: through judgment, toward Zion. Hold that shape; chapter 24 is about to blow it up to cosmic size.

Application

  • About God: he has purposed to humble "all the honored of the earth" (23:9), and he can consecrate the wages of a city he judged (23:18). No economy is beyond his verdict, and none beyond his redemption.
  • About you: Tyre's temptation is the respectable one: not idols, just margins; merchants as princes. Ask where your earning or spending has become "prostitution" in Isaiah's sense: intimacy with anything for a fee, profit with no conscience attached.
  • Do: practice 23:18 in miniature this week. Take a real portion of one paycheck or one sale and make it "holy to the LORD": not stored or hoarded, but food and provision for those who serve before him. Decide the amount before Sunday.
