Lesson 0033 · Unit 4: Chapters 24-27

Isaiah 24: The Earth Laid Waste

~30 min · Passage: Isaiah 24:1-23 (ESV, read first!)

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 24 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. As you read, ask one question the whole way through: who exactly is being judged in this chapter? Keep your answer beside you; it is the door into a new unit.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • No nation named: after eleven chapters of oracles against Babylon, Assyria, Philistia, Moab, Damascus, Egypt, Tyre, this chapter judges... no one in particular. "Behold, the LORD will empty the earth" (24:1). The whole earth.
  • Every class leveled (24:2): people and priest, slave and master, buyer and seller, lender and borrower. Judgment ignores every social rank.
  • The reason given (24:5): "they have transgressed the laws, violated the statutes, broken the everlasting covenant." A legal charge against the earth itself.
  • The wine fails (24:7-9): the party dies mid-song. "No more do they drink wine with singing."
  • A nameless city (24:10): "the wasted city is broken down." Which city? Isaiah does not say.
  • Two songs at once (24:14-16): songs of glory rise "from the ends of the earth," yet the prophet cries "I waste away, I waste away. Woe is me!" (24:16).
  • A staggering earth (24:19-20): "the earth staggers like a drunken man; it sways like a hut." Creation itself reels.
  • Judgment above and below (24:21): "the LORD will punish the host of heaven, in heaven, and the kings of the earth, on the earth."
  • Prison, then punishment (24:22): gathered "as prisoners in a pit... and after many days they will be punished." A strange two-stage sequence.
  • The last word is a throne (24:23): "the LORD of hosts reigns on Mount Zion and in Jerusalem, and his glory will be before his elders." The chapter of ruin ends in reign.

Interpretation

From the nations to the earth

Chapters 13-23 worked through the nations one by one, each oracle carrying its own address label. Chapter 24 tears the labels off. The judgment is now "the earth," a word Isaiah repeats about sixteen times in this one chapter, and every rank of society falls together (24:2). Chapters 24-27 are often called "Isaiah's apocalypse": the same themes as the oracles (pride brought low, a city broken, Zion exalted) but painted on a canvas the size of the world. Think of chapters 13-23 as case-by-case verdicts and chapter 24 as the closing argument: what God did to each nation in turn, he will do to the whole rebellious order at once.

Why the whole earth? The broken covenant

The earth lies defiled under its inhabitants; for they have transgressed the laws, violated the statutes, broken the everlasting covenant. Isaiah 24:5, ESV

Here is the legal ground for a worldwide sentence. Which covenant can "the earth" break? Not Sinai; the nations never stood at that mountain. The best candidate is the covenant God made with Noah and with "every living creature... for all future generations" (Genesis 9:9-16), the covenant that binds all humanity to its Maker and even there assumes basic law: a reckoning for bloodshed (Genesis 9:5-6). Paul argues the same way in Romans 1-2: even those without the law of Moses know enough of God's decree to be "without excuse." So no nation, and no person, can plead exemption. The earth is not judged arbitrarily; it is judged for breach of a covenant everyone is inside.

The city of chaos

"The wasted city is broken down; every house is shut up so that none can enter" (24:10). The Hebrew phrase is close to "city of tohu," the "without form" word of Genesis 1:2: a city of chaos, unmade. Isaiah leaves it unnamed on purpose. You have already met this city wearing masks: Babylon in chapter 13, Babylon fallen in 21:9, Tyre the merchant city in 23. The nameless city is the world-city, human civilization organized in pride against God, whatever flag it flies in a given century. Its songs stop (24:8-9); Revelation borrows exactly this picture for its "Babylon the great," where "the sound of harpists and musicians... will be heard in you no more" (Revelation 18:22). Against it stands the other city, and the chapter ends there.

Judgment above and below, then a throne

Verse 21 widens the frame beyond anything in the oracles: "the host of heaven, in heaven, and the kings of the earth, on the earth." Rebellion has a spiritual layer as well as a political one, and judgment reaches both (compare Ephesians 6:12). Then the odd detail of 24:22: the rebels are "gathered together as prisoners in a pit... shut up in a prison, and after many days they will be punished." Confinement first, final sentence later. Revelation 20:1-10 echoes this shape (binding, then after a period, final judgment), and each eschatology reads that echo differently, as the fork below will show. But do not miss where the chapter lands: "the LORD of hosts reigns on Mount Zion" (24:23). The sun and moon are ashamed, not because light loses, but because a greater glory outshines them. When the world-city falls, Zion is still standing, and the LORD is on the throne in front of his elders (compare Exodus 24:9-11, where elders also see God and eat with him; hold that thought for chapter 25).

Eschatology fork #3: how to read apocalyptic

This is the course's third marked fork, after 2:1-5 (lesson 0005) and 11:1-10 (lesson 0020). Before choosing a lens, name the genre. Apocalyptic language uses cosmic, earth-wide imagery (staggering earth, darkened sun) for God's decisive judgments both within history and at its end; Isaiah has already used "the stars will not give their light" for Babylon's fall (13:10). And notice what chapters 24-27 lack: a date stamp. Moab got "in three years" (16:14), Kedar got "within a year" (21:16); this section gets "in that day," open-ended. So the readings differ (see the glossary on eschatology):

  • MacArthur (premillennial): chapters 24-27 describe the future tribulation, and 24:23 the millennial kingdom that follows Christ's return; the "after many days" of 24:22 is the gap between Satan's binding and the final judgment of Revelation 20. Read his notes on this chapter in your copy.
  • White (amillennial): the imagery spans the whole age between Christ's two advents; the world-city keeps falling in history's judgments, and the one return of Christ brings resurrection and final judgment together. The 24:22 sequence is a picture, not a timeline.
  • Durbin (postmillennial, our home lens): God's world-judgments land within history as the kingdom advances (Babylon fell, Rome fell, every world-city falls), and 24:23, the LORD reigning on Zion, describes the gospel age itself, Christ reigning now (1 Corinthians 15:25), with the final judgment still ahead at the end.

Argued from the text: the postmil and amil readings can point to 13:10, where cosmic language served an in-history fall, and to the absent date stamp fitting a pattern rather than one event. The premil reading can point to the two-stage prison sequence of 24:22, which does map strikingly onto Revelation 20. Honestly, this chapter underdetermines the choice; all three lenses agree on what the learner must not miss: the whole earth answers to God, the world-city falls, and the LORD reigns from Zion. Hold the fork open; chapters 25-27 add more evidence.

Application

  • About God: his authority is not regional. Every rank, every nation, every spiritual power answers to him (24:2, 21). There is no neutral ground and no exempt person, including you, and no power over you that is not itself under him.
  • About you: the world-city's songs are loud until the moment they stop (24:8). Ask where your security is invested: in an order that staggers like a drunkard, or in the city whose King reigns (24:23)? Name one "city of chaos" comfort you lean on too hard.
  • Do: read Genesis 9:1-17 tonight beside Isaiah 24:5, and write one sentence on why the whole earth can be held to covenant. Bring it up in your church conversation this week: most believers have never asked which covenant the nations broke.
