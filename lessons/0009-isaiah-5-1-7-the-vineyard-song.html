Lesson 0009 · Unit 1: Chapters 1-6

Isaiah 5:1-7: The Song of the Vineyard

~25 min · Passage: Isaiah 5:1-7 (ESV, read first!) · A love song with a trapdoor

Did you read it?
If you have not yet read Isaiah 5:1-7 twice and written five observations, stop here and do it now. Especially: list every single thing the owner did for the vineyard, and mark the verse where the song stops being a song.

Recall first

Observation check

Compare your list. Strong observations on this passage include:

  • The form (v. 1): "Let me sing for my beloved my love song concerning his vineyard." Isaiah steps up like a minstrel at a festival. The audience settles in for entertainment.
  • Total provision (vv. 1-2): a very fertile hill; dug; cleared of stones; choice vines; a watchtower built; a wine vat hewn. Six completed actions before a single grape. Nothing more could be done (v. 4 says exactly that).
  • The anticlimax (v. 2): "he looked for it to yield grapes, but it yielded wild grapes". Sour, worthless fruit despite perfect conditions.
  • The audience subpoenaed (v. 3): "And now, O inhabitants of Jerusalem and men of Judah, judge between me and my vineyard." The listeners are suddenly the jury.
  • The unanswerable question (v. 4): "What more was there to do for my vineyard, that I have not done in it?"
  • The sentence (vv. 5-6): hedge removed, wall broken, no pruning, no rain. Judgment here is mostly withdrawal: God stops protecting and stops tending.
  • The reveal (v. 7): "the vineyard of the LORD of hosts is the house of Israel". The jury discovers it has convicted itself.
  • The wordplay (v. 7): "he looked for justice, but behold, bloodshed; for righteousness, but behold, an outcry!" Even in English the lines land like a slap; in Hebrew they rhyme.

Interpretation

The Nathan maneuver

The song is a trap of grace, the same move Nathan used on David: tell a story that recruits the hearer's own sense of justice, then spring "You are the man" (2 Sam 12:1-7). By v. 4 every honest listener has already agreed the vineyard deserves nothing. Verse 7 closes the door: the vineyard is Israel, "the men of Judah are his pleasant planting". Self-condemnation out of one's own mouth is the most unarguable verdict there is. Watch for this technique; the prophets and Jesus both love it, because self-righteousness cannot be argued down, only ambushed.

The pun in verse 7

...and he looked for justice, but behold, bloodshed; for righteousness, but behold, an outcry! Isaiah 5:7, ESV

In Hebrew the expected words and the delivered words sound almost identical. God looked for mishpat (justice) and got mishpach (bloodshed); he looked for tsedaqah (righteousness) and got tse'aqah (an outcry, the scream of the oppressed). An English approximation: he looked for right dealing and found blood spilling; he sought equity and heard agony. The fruit God wanted from his vineyard was never ritual performance; chapter 1 already established that. It was justice and righteousness lived out among his people. The wild grapes are the crushed poor of 3:14-15.

Jesus sings the second verse

In Matthew 21:33-46 Jesus retells this exact song to the chief priests: same vineyard, same fence, tower, and wine vat, but now the owner sends servants (the prophets) and finally his son, whom the tenants kill. The leaders answer his "what will the owner do?" with their own self-condemnation, then realize "he was speaking about them" (Matt 21:45): the Nathan maneuver again, from the greater Nathan. His sentence: "the kingdom of God will be taken away from you and given to a people producing its fruits" (21:43). The vineyard is not abandoned; it changes tenants. The fruit requirement stands over the church now, which is why the New Testament keeps talking about fruit (John 15:1-8, Gal 5:22-23).

Application

  • About God: v. 4 is his self-defense, and it is unanswerable. When judgment falls, the failure is never in God's provision. Count the six completed actions again and then count his provisions for you.
  • About you: privilege is not fruit. Judah had the hill, the hedge, and the tower and produced an outcry. Christian upbringing, sound church, good theology: all vineyard equipment. The owner still walks in looking for grapes: justice, righteousness, the Spirit's fruit.
  • Ask concretely: if Jesus inspected your last month for the fruit of 5:7, justice and righteousness in how you treated people, what would he find? Name one wild grape to uproot this week.
